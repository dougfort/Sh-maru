# 秋丸 Shūmaru

---

## Chapter 7 The Autumn

The summer did not end so much as it withdrew — pulling back by degrees, the heat thinning first at the edges of the day, the mornings arriving cool again before the afternoons had given up their warmth. The cicadas continued for a while after the quality of the air had changed. Then they stopped, and their stopping made audible how quiet the mountain already was.

The maples at the clearing's edge began to turn in the middle of the eighth month. Not all at once — first one, then three, then the whole edge of the gap catching the change and holding it, the leaves moving through yellow and orange and into the deep red that the mountain did best. The cryptomeria stayed as they were, dark and permanent, indifferent to the season's insistence. Between them, glimpsed through the trunks on the path, the colored edges burned.

Shūmaru went to the circle.

He went as he had always gone — in the early morning, before the household woke, following the path through the shrine and up into the older trees. The ground was changing underfoot: the needle-fall drier, more brittle, the occasional maple leaf beginning to appear among the dark cryptomeria duff, vivid and temporary. The stream was at its lowest, the pale stones of the bed exposed more widely than at any other time of year, the water narrowed to a thread at the center of what had been a broader current in spring.

He sat on his stone. The pool was shallow now, its surface perfectly still except where the reduced current entered it. He could see every stone on the bottom, each one distinct, nothing obscured. The reflection was very clear.

He put his hand in the water. Cold — colder than the air, as always, the mountain's deep cold preserved in it regardless of the season's surface warmth. The current pressed against his fingers, gentler now than in spring but still purposeful, still going somewhere.

The *shinki* was present. It was always present here — that accumulated silence, the quality of attention in the air that was not directed at him but simply was, the way the mountain was. He had never arrived to find it absent. He did not expect to.

He sat for a long time. The light moved across the clearing as the morning advanced, the autumn sun low enough now to enter the gap at a different angle than it had in summer, throwing longer shadows from the dacite columns, making the geometry of their facets more pronounced. He watched the shadows move. He watched the water. He watched the light.

She was not there.

He had known she would not be. He had known this since the solstice morning — not as a conclusion he had reached but as something he had simply woken up understanding, the way you wake up understanding that it has snowed in the night before you have opened your eyes. The figure belonged to the solstice. The circle had been built for that morning. One specific dawn in the year, and then the year turning, and the circle returning to what it always was: old stones, cold water, a presence that required nothing and offered everything.

He was not grieving exactly. Grief was for something that should have been otherwise. This was not that. This was simply what it was — the world continuing in its correct sequence, the summer spent, the autumn arrived, the light angled differently through the same gap in the canopy.

But he was different from who he had been in spring.

He did not examine this difference directly. To examine it would be to grasp at it, and grasping was not the correct relationship with what he was carrying. He simply sat with it, the way he sat with everything, and let it be what it was.

---

The riding master had noticed the change before anyone else. Or if others had noticed — his mother, perhaps, with her particular quality of seeing him — they had said nothing, which was the correct response. The riding master was not a man for silence. On an afternoon in the early ninth month, when Shūmaru had been sitting too still on the mare's back, letting her find her own pace rather than directing it, the man said without particular gentleness:

*Where are you?*

Shūmaru came back from wherever he had been. He gathered the reins properly, corrected his posture, applied the correct pressure with his heels. The mare responded immediately, her ears flicking back in acknowledgment.

*Here*, he said.

The riding master looked at him for a moment with the expression he used when he was deciding whether to say more. He decided not to. The lesson continued.

That evening Shūmaru thought about the question. *Where are you?* Not a question about his body, which had been precisely where it should have been. A question about something else — the quality of his presence on the horse, the degree to which he was attending to the animal beneath him and the ground ahead rather than to whatever occupied him in the place no one could see.

He had no answer that would have satisfied the riding master. He was not sure the question had a satisfying answer.

---

The moon-viewing came in the middle of the eighth month — *tsukimi*, the harvest moon, the full moon considered most beautiful of the year. The household arranged it correctly: *susuki* grass in a tall vase on the veranda, the round rice dumplings (*tsukimi dango*) piled in their pyramid on a lacquered stand, the garden oriented toward the rising east so that the moon could be received properly when it came.

They sat on the veranda — Ume, Mitsumaru, Shūmaru, the household disposed in its usual order — and waited for the moon to clear the mountain ridge above the estate.

It came slowly, the way the moon always came: first a brightening at the ridge's edge, then the first curved sliver of it, then more, then the full circle rising free of the trees and the rock and hanging above everything, enormous and present, the valley below flooded in its light so that the mist, if there had been mist, would have been luminous. There was no mist tonight. The sky was entirely clear, the moon so bright it cast distinct shadows from the shaped pines, from the stone lantern, from the three of them sitting on the veranda.

*It's very large this year*, Mitsumaru said. He was not wrong — it did seem large. Perhaps it was always large and they only noticed when they were required to sit still and attend to it.

Ume said nothing. She looked at the moon with a quality of attention Shūmaru recognized — it was, he sometimes thought, where he had learned to look at things the way he looked at things. Not from his father, who looked at things instrumentally, gauging their use or significance. From her.

Shūmaru looked at the moon reflected in the garden pond. A compressed version of it, very white, slightly blurred at the edges where the water was not quite still. He had seen the moon reflected in the pond at the circle earlier that week — in the shallow pool, the moon small and exact, the water cold and clear and perfectly level. Two reflections of the same thing, in different water, differently distilled.

He held this without quite knowing what to do with it.

Later, in his room, a line arrived:

*Still water holds the moon—*

He didn't continue it. He turned it over once and set it aside. It was not right yet. It was the shape of something that had not yet found its form. He let it rest where it was and went to sleep.

---

The autumn deepened. The maples came into their full color and then began to let it go — the leaves dropping one at a time at first, then in small sudden cascades when the wind moved through, the path to the shrine acquiring a new texture of red and yellow over the dark needle-fall. He noticed the specific morning when the first leaf fell into the circle's pool — a maple leaf, deeply red, spinning twice on the current and then slipping around the stone and away downstream. He watched it go.

In the lesson room the texts continued. Gen'nosuke set him a passage from the *Man'yōshū* — the oldest Japanese poetry anthology, its language archaic and dense — and Shūmaru worked through it slowly, parsing the ancient syllables. The poem was about autumn and the mountain and something passing. He copied it three times without being asked to.

Gen'nosuke watched him copy it the third time and said nothing.

After the lesson, at the door, he paused.

*The circle*, he said. Not a question.

*Yes*, Shūmaru said.

That was all. Gen'nosuke went to his room. Shūmaru stood in the corridor for a moment, aware of what had just passed between them without any of it having been said. Then he went to his own room and sat at his desk and looked at the *Man'yōshū* passage he had copied three times and thought about the leaf in the pool and the way the light was now in the clearing and whether he had the right word yet.

He didn't. But he was closer.

---

The ninth month brought the autumn equinox — *higan* again, the Buddhist observance that balanced the spring version, graves visited, offerings made, the family's continuity with its dead maintained through the correct forms. Shūmaru participated correctly, as he always did. He stood at the right place. He bowed at the right moments. He carried what he was asked to carry.

At the family graves, waiting while his mother arranged the offerings, he looked at the characters carved into the oldest stones — names worn to near-smoothness by years of weather, barely legible, the people they marked entirely dissolved into the mountain and its seasons. A time before anything he had been taught to think about, his mother had said once, speaking of the estate's history. He had not then known what that phrase might contain. He thought he understood it better now.

The evening after the higan visit he sat on the veranda in the cooling dark and watched the valley below fill slowly with the first real mist of autumn — not the summer haze, which erased through excess of heat, but the true mist, which erased through something older and more patient. The valley disappeared in layers, the furthest slopes first, then the nearer ones, then the paddies and the river, until only the mountain on which he sat remained visible, the estate floating above a white sea as it had on the equinox morning that had begun everything.

He watched the mist rise.

Two more lines arrived, and then nothing:

*— the figure gone.*
*What remains is not absence.*

He turned these over for a long time. They were not haiku yet — too long, too explanatory, too much the mind working on what the mind could not reach. He set them aside again.

Somewhere in the deeper passages of the house Gen'nosuke's shakuhachi began — the notes arriving through the corridors with their usual quality of deliberateness, each note complete before the next. Shūmaru listened to it from the veranda in the dark and the mist until the cold finally moved him inside.

He was carrying what he was carrying. He was learning how.
