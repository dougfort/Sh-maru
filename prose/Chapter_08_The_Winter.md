# 秋丸 Shūmaru

---

## Chapter 8 The Winter

Snow came to the upper slopes in the tenth month and to the estate itself three weeks later — not gradually, as the autumn had come, but in the way mountain snow came: a night of wind, a morning of stillness, and then the world remade in white. The garden disappeared. The shaped pines became different shapes, their trained branches holding the snow in accumulated masses until, one by one through the day, they released it in sudden soft falls that Shūmaru could hear from his room — a muffled weight, then silence, then the branch springing back.

The path to the shrine was his alone now. This was a thing he had always known in an abstract way — that in winter no one else came this far, that his were the only footprints — but this year he understood it differently. Not as solitude, which was simply his natural condition. As something more like custodianship. He went to the shrine and cleared the snow from the offering box, from the step before it, from the rope and its streamers. He brought his offering. He stood in the silence.

He did not go to the circle.

Not yet. The path above the shrine was buried deep. He could have found it — had found it before in darkness and rain, and snow was only another kind of darkness in a certain sense. But something in him was not ready. The circle was there. The stones were under the snow. The stream was running beneath its ice, audible if you stood at the right place and listened. He could wait.

He was good at waiting. He had always been good at waiting.

---

The lesson room was colder in winter than in any other season — the north-facing window, the inadequate hibachi, the particular cold that settled into the tatami this side of the house and did not shift until spring. Gen'nosuke taught in the same quality of stillness regardless of temperature. Shūmaru had observed that his tutor did not seem to feel the cold in the way the household's servants did — did not rub his hands or draw his robes tighter. He simply occupied the cold room as he occupied everything, without friction.

The texts continued. In the eleventh month they began a new section of the *Analects* — the passages about the gentleman, the *junzi*, the ideal cultivated person. Shūmaru had studied these before, in earlier books, receiving them as moral instruction, understanding their importance without feeling particularly addressed by them. This time something in them snagged.

*The Master said: At fifteen I had my mind bent on learning. At thirty I stood firm. At forty I had no doubts. At fifty I knew the decrees of Heaven. At sixty my ear was an obedient organ for the reception of truth. At seventy I could follow what my heart desired, without transgressing what was right.*

He copied it carefully. He read it back. He sat with it.

Gen'nosuke was watching him — the quality of divided attention Shūmaru had long since learned to read. He did not look up.

*Sensei*, he said. *At what age did Confucius mean — the decrees of Heaven?*

*Fifty*, Gen'nosuke said. *The passage says fifty.*

*I mean —* Shūmaru paused, finding the question that was actually underneath the question. *What does it mean to know the decrees of Heaven? Not to understand them. To know them.*

Gen'nosuke was quiet for a moment. When he answered, his voice had the careful quality it took on when he was not teaching from a text but from somewhere else.

*I think*, he said slowly, *it means to have received something that cannot be argued with. Something that arrives without being sent for. You do not decide to know it. It simply becomes known.*

Shūmaru looked at his inkstone. The ink had dried slightly at the edges.

*And before fifty?* he asked.

*Before fifty*, Gen'nosuke said, *there is learning. And standing firm. And having no doubts — which is not the same as knowing.* A pause. *Confucius was describing a life. Not a prescription. He was being accurate about what had happened to him, in what order.*

Shūmaru nodded once. He ground a little more water into the inkstone and continued copying.

The passage sat in him for the rest of the day, and the day after, and several days after that. Something had arrived without being sent for. He was not fifty. He was ten, eleven by the reckoning that counted him one year older at New Year. But he thought he understood, now, what Confucius had meant. Or understood something adjacent to it. Something that had come to him in a clearing on a summer morning and had not left.

---

It was on an evening in the eleventh month, when the snow was deep and the household had drawn inward around the warmth of the kitchen and the braziers, that Shūmaru went to sit with his mother.

Not for any particular reason. Not because something had happened that required him to go to her. He had simply finished his reading and stood up and walked to her room, which was not a thing he did often or by habit, and found her there at her low desk with her household accounts spread before her — the columns of figures, the careful record of the estate's winter provisions, the numbers that kept Kiri no Ue functioning in his father's continued absence.

She did not look surprised to see him. She moved a candle slightly so that its light reached the room's corner where he settled himself, cross-legged on the tatami with his back against the wall. She returned to her accounts.

They sat in this way for some time. The house made its winter sounds around them — the deep timbers contracting in the cold, a distant sound of wind in the cryptomeria, the particular quality of silence that snow outside a house produced. Ume's brush moved in its small, efficient strokes across the page. Shūmaru watched the candle.

After a while she said, without looking up: *The plum will be early this year.*

He considered this. She knew the plum's rhythms the way he knew the forest — specifically, by accumulated attention rather than any single observation. *How do you know?* he asked.

*The cold came late and then all at once. When it comes that way, the plum thinks it has already waited long enough.* She turned a page. *It will bloom before the snow is gone.*

He thought about this — the plum blooming in snow, the first color against all that white, its particular quality of insistence. Her name-flower. He had always found it apt, though he had never said so.

*Does it —* he began, and stopped.

She waited. She was very good at waiting. Better than he was, perhaps, which was saying something.

*Does it know*, he said carefully, *that it might snow again after it blooms? That it might be covered again?*

She set down her brush. She looked at him then — directly, the full quality of her attention, which was not a comfortable thing to receive but was not an uncomfortable one either. It was simply precise.

*No*, she said. *I don't think it knows. It blooms because it is time to bloom. What comes after is not its concern.*

He sat with this.

*Is that —* he started again, and again stopped.

*Go on*, she said gently.

*Is that not frightening?* He was aware that this was not quite the question he meant, but it was as close as he could come. *To bloom without knowing?*

She was quiet for a moment. Not assembling an answer — he knew the difference between Gen'nosuke assembling a scholarly answer and his mother waiting to say what she already knew.

*I think*, she said, *that frightening is not the right word for it. There is something it requires, yes. A willingness to be present to the moment without requiring the moment to be safe.* She paused. *Your father would call it foolishness. Gen'nosuke might find a better word for it.*

*What would you call it?*

She considered. *Correct*, she said. *I would call it correct.*

The word landed with a particular weight. Shūmaru felt it in the way you feel a word that has been used before in a significant context, arriving again in a different one. He had used that word himself, in the lesson room, about the circle. Gen'nosuke had received it with his characteristic stillness. And now his mother had arrived at it independently, from a different direction entirely.

He looked at the candle. He did not say anything.

Ume returned to her accounts. The brush moved in its small strokes. The house made its sounds around them.

After another long interval she said, without emphasis: *You have been different since the summer.*

*Yes.*

*You don't have to tell me about it.*

*I know.*

She turned another page. *But if you want to—*

*Not yet*, he said. And then, because this sounded like a door closing: *Sometime.*

She nodded once, in the way he recognized as his own gesture returned to him. Then she went back to her accounts, and he sat with the candle, and the winter settled around the house, and the plum somewhere outside in the frozen garden was already, invisibly, deciding.

---

The poem arrived not as a whole but as a clearing. He woke one morning in the twelfth month — the sky still dark, the house silent — and lay beneath the yogi and understood that the fragments he had been accumulating were not pieces waiting to be assembled. They were the residue of approaches that had not worked. What the poem actually was had been present all along, as simple and clear as the circle's pool in autumn, and all the fragments had been the mind trying to think its way to something that could only be received.

He lay still for a long time.

Then he rose and went to his desk and ground his ink slowly, in the circular motion that was its own kind of thinking, and when the ink was ready he wrote it down.

Seventeen syllables. He read it back once. He set down his brush.

It was right. Not because it said everything — it said almost nothing, in the way a circle drawn in a single stroke said almost nothing and contained everything. It was right because it was finished. The way a circle was finished.

He folded the paper carefully and put it in the sleeve of his robe, next to his skin. He did not reread it. He did not show it to anyone.

Outside the window the snow had come again in the night, covering the garden, covering the path, covering whatever lay up the slope in the deeper forest. The plum's branches held the snow in the gray pre-dawn light, bare and black and patient. He looked at them for a moment.

Then he turned from the window and went to begin the day.
