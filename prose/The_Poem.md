# 秋丸 Shūmaru
## The Poem — 俳句

---

### The Poem in Japanese

&nbsp;

# 寒池に
# 円欠けたるや
# 過ぎしもの

&nbsp;

---

### Romanization

*Kan ike ni*
*En kaketa ru ya*
*Sugishi mono*

---

### The English Version

*Mist on the cold pool —*
*an unfinished circle holds*
*what passed through once.*

---

### Notes on the Japanese

The Japanese poem works as a traditional haiku in 5-7-5 *mora* — the Japanese sound units that govern the rhythm of classical haiku. Mora are not syllables in the English sense; each kana character represents one mora, making the counting precise.

**Line one — 寒池に** (5 mora: *ka-n-i-ke-ni*)
- 寒 *kan* — cold, winter cold. This is the *kigo*, the seasonal word that places the poem in winter.
- 池 *ike* — pond, pool
- に *ni* — particle indicating location: *at*, *on*, *by*
- Together: *on/by the cold pool*

**Line two — 円欠けたるや** (7 mora: *e-n-ka-ke-ta-ru-ya*)
- 円 *en* — circle. Also: completeness, wholeness. The character used in ensō — 円相 — the Zen brushed circle.
- 欠けたる *kaketaru* — incomplete, broken, lacking. Classical form of 欠けた.
- や *ya* — the *kireji*, the cutting word. A pause, a breath, a gap. It cuts the poem in two and holds the two halves in relation without explaining the relation. The ensō's open gap, rendered in grammar.
- Together: *the circle, incomplete —*

**Line three — 過ぎしもの** (5 mora: *su-gi-shi-mo-no*)
- 過ぎし *sugishi* — that which has passed, classical past tense of 過ぎる (*sugiru*, to pass through, to go by)
- もの *mono* — thing, that which, what. An indefinite noun that holds the unnamed.
- Together: *what has passed through* / *what passed*

---

### The Kireji — や

The cutting word や (*ya*) in the second line performs the poem's essential work. It creates the gap — the *ma* — between the unfinished circle and what passed through it. It does not explain the relationship. It holds the two images in proximity and lets the reader feel the connection without being told what it is.

This is the ensō in grammar: the circle almost closed, the gap where something entered or left, the space that makes the whole possible.

---

### The Ensō Connection

The character 円 (*en*) appears in:
- **円相 *ensō*** — the Zen brushed circle, symbol of emptiness and wholeness
- **秋丸 Shūmaru** — the boy's name: 秋 *shū* (autumn) + 丸 *maru* (circle, wholeness)
- **The stone circle** — the Jōmon *iwasaka*, the incomplete arc of buried stones
- **The poem itself** — seventeen sounds in three lines, returning to silence

The poem is the ensō. Complete and not complete. Open enough for something to have passed through.

---

### As It Appears in the Story

The poem is written on a small piece of paper, folded carefully, tucked into a crevice of the columnar dacite at the stone circle on the morning of the winter solstice — the darkest day, the year's turning point. Weighted with a smooth pale stone carried up from the stream.

Gen'nosuke finds it weeks or months later, in spring, the winter having softened one corner of the paper, the ink blurred where the moisture reached it. He reads what remains. He leaves it where it is.

---

*Written by 霧山秋丸 Kiriyama Shūmaru, age eleven.*
*Left at the stone circle, winter solstice, circa 1700.*
