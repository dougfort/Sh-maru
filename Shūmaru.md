# 秋丸 Shūmaru
## Story Bible

---

### Title
**秋丸 Shūmaru**

---

### Setting
**Period:** Middle Edo, approximately 1688–1720 — overlapping with the Genroku era, Japan's cultural peak
**Location:** 霧の上 *Kiri no Ue* ("above the mist") — the Kiriyama family estate, mountain country of central Honshū, old province of Shinano (modern Nagano prefecture)
**Atmosphere:** Ordered and constrained. Quiet. Ancient in feeling. Mist is a recurring presence — erasing boundaries, suspending the ordinary world.

---

### The Estate — 霧の上 Kiri no Ue
*See separate Estate document for full detail.*
- Situated on a mountain slope above a mist-filled valley
- Old cryptomeria forest behind; rice paddies and farming villages below, largely invisible in morning mist
- The Kiriyama family — 霧山, *mist* + *mountain* — have occupied this estate for generations
- The family maintains an ancient Inari shrine in the forest above the garden — older than the house, older than anyone's clear memory
- Above the shrine, deeper in the forest: the Jōmon stone circle — older than the shrine, older than any name currently given to what dwells there
- **Mist** is central to the story's atmosphere — it erases boundaries, creates privacy, belongs to the threshold times of dawn and certain summer evenings

---

### The Story's Shape

**秋丸 Shūmaru** follows a boy through approximately one year of his life — from spring through the following winter solstice. It is structured as a year in the life of a boy of his age and class: richly observed, full of period detail, the rituals and seasons and lessons and obligations of his world. This is the story's surface — exotic, beautiful, meticulously grounded.

Running underneath, quietly: a fox. A stone circle. A glimpse.

**The arc:**

**Spring** — the world waking. Shūmaru's year begins. The valley becomes visible as mist thins. The estate comes alive. His days resume their structured rhythm — lessons, martial training, the garden, the horses. On the spring equinox morning, going further into the forest than he has gone before, following the sound of the stream, he finds the stone circle. He recognizes it immediately as a place of *shinki* — sacred presence — older than the shrine below. He returns repeatedly through spring, telling no one.

**Early summer** — the forest at its deepest green. Shūmaru returns repeatedly to the circle. Through his growing trust with Gen'nosuke, he shares the discovery — not as mystery, simply as a place he has found. Gen'nosuke gives him language: the kami, the old ways, the nature of sacred ground. Their relationship deepens around this shared attention to the spirit world, without either of them naming what it is becoming. Gen'nosuke recognizes the site from his dead teacher's notes, but keeps this to himself. He is finally close to what he has been looking for — and wise enough to move carefully.

**Midsummer — 夏至 *Geshi*, the summer solstice** — the year at its fullest. Shūmaru goes to the stone circle before dawn. The mist is different this morning — heavier, more luminous. And in the circle, through the mist, a figure. A girl, approximately his age, dancing **神楽 *kagura*** among the stones — alone, performing an offering to whatever inhabits that place. Slow in places, suddenly quick. Her arms carrying gestures whose weight he feels without being able to read their meaning.

He stands at the tree line. Completely still. He watches.

The mist is such that he cannot be completely certain of what he sees. The figure is not quite solid. The moment is not quite real in the ordinary sense.

The dance ends. She is still. Then — without turning — she speaks to him. Or he thinks she does. The mist and the dawn make certainty impossible.

He is deeply, permanently affected. Something has passed through him and changed the shape of what he is. He cannot return to being simply a boy on an estate in the mountains. The glimpse, uncertain as it was, is more real than most things he has known with certainty. Like the Kurosawa boy who witnesses what he should not — he is cut off from his previous experience. Not by loss. By vision.

**Autumn** — Shūmaru's season. The maples turning, the light becoming particular — clear, slightly melancholy. The mist returns to the valley. He goes to the stone circle. Nothing. He sits on his stone. The stream runs cold. The *shinki* is present as always — steady, old, undirected. But the figure does not appear again.

He does not tell Gen'nosuke about the figure. He holds it privately. He composes lines of poetry in his head, discards them, begins again.

The autumn is beautiful and costs him something. *Mono no aware* — the pathos of things, the beauty of impermanence — is no longer an aesthetic concept. It is the specific texture of his days.

**Winter** — the estate turns inward. Snow buries the garden. His footprints in the path are the only ones. The stone circle is half-buried. He goes there less — not because he has given up, but because the giving up is happening slowly and internally, the way things happen in him. He has the poem almost complete.

**Winter solstice — 冬至 *Tōji*** — the darkest day, the year's turning point. The moment of maximum darkness before the light begins its slow return. He goes to the stone circle one last time. Snow is deep. The dacite columns are black against white. The stream runs under ice at its edges.

He sits on his stone. He stays for a long time.

He leaves the poem at the circle — tucked into a crevice of the columnar dacite. Weighted with a stone.

He walks back through the cryptomeria. He does not look back.

He never returns to the circle. Not because he is forbidden to, not because something prevents him. Because the poem was the closing of something, and you do not reopen what has been properly closed.

The light is already, incrementally, returning.

### The Glimpse

Shūmaru witnesses the figure at the solstice, before full dawn, through mist. He cannot be completely certain of what he sees. This uncertainty is permanent and deliberate — the story never resolves it.

What he saw may have been the kitsune of the mountain. Or something older, less named. Or a vision produced by the particular quality of the solstice dawn and the ancient ground.

The story does not adjudicate. Neither does Shūmaru. He simply holds what he saw, turns it over privately, and eventually makes it into a poem.

**What she said** — if she said anything — is not recorded in the story. It belongs to the mist.

---

### The Poem

A haiku, composed over the autumn and winter. Left at the stone circle on the winter solstice.

**Not yet written.** It will arrive at the story's end, when we know exactly what the story has been.

What it must hold: the circle. Complete and not complete. Open enough for something to have passed through.

The character 円 (*en*) — circle, complete, round — may or may not appear directly. But its quality must be present. The ensō, brushed in a single stroke, the gap where the circle almost meets itself.

### Characters

#### 秋丸 Shūmaru (protagonist)
- Boy, eight or nine at the story's opening, around ten at the solstice
- Son of the Kiriyama family — high-ranking samurai, hatamoto or senior retainer class
- Slight, solemn, serious-faced
- Observant and watchful, with a rich interior life
- Solitary by temperament — most himself when alone
- Highly intelligent
- Perceives the kami naturally, without thinking of it as unusual
- Carries his childhood name 秋丸 throughout
  - 秋 *shū* — autumn
  - 丸 *maru* — circle, wholeness

**His arc:** The story changes him. The glimpse at the solstice cuts him off from his previous experience — not as loss, but as vision. The autumn and winter are his interior process of accepting this, and eventually closing it properly with the poem. He moves into whatever comes next — different, carrying something that cannot be named but cannot be lost.

---

#### 霧山 Ume — Shūmaru's mother
- 梅 *Ume* — plum blossom. Blooms in late winter while snow is still on the ground.
- Manages the estate with quiet authority during her husband's absences
- Warm but not intrusive — she gives Shūmaru room while watching carefully
- The one adult who comes closest to seeing him clearly
- She notices the change in him after the solstice. She does not ask. She leaves food outside his door on cold mornings.

---

#### 霧山 — Shūmaru's father
- Name to be determined
- High-ranking, periodically absent under the sankin-kōtai system
- Formal, correct. His attention is organized around Mitsumaru, the heir.
- Not cold — simply a man whose instruments cannot measure what Shūmaru is.

---

#### 光丸 Mitsumaru — Shūmaru's older brother
- 光丸 — *light* + *maru*. Five years older.
- The heir. Confident, warm in direct contact, oblivious to Shūmaru's interior world.
- Moves through Kiri no Ue as if it were made for him — because it was.
- The two brothers: 光丸 light, 秋丸 autumn.

---

#### 玄之介 Gen'nosuke — Shūmaru's tutor
*See separate character sheet for full detail.*
- Formal name: 玄之介 Gen'nosuke. Scholarly name: 静庵 Seian.
- Late forties. Local origin. Kyoto-educated — Confucian classics, Zen at Daitoku-ji, deep Shinto scholarship.
- Drawn to Kiri no Ue by his dead teacher's investigation of the shrine. Has been looking for the stone circle from the wrong direction.

**His role in this story:** When Shūmaru shares the stone circle, Gen'nosuke gives him language for what he perceives — the kami, the old ways, the nature of sacred ground — without explaining away the mystery. He does not learn about the figure at the solstice. What Shūmaru holds privately remains private. But Gen'nosuke gives him the tools to hold it with.

After the solstice he notices the change in Shūmaru. He does not press. He offers a kōan once, quietly, and lets it stand.

He eventually finds the stone circle — after Shūmaru has left the poem and stopped going. He finds the poem, weather-worn, in the crevice of the dacite. He cannot read it fully. He leaves it where it is.

---

#### The Kitsune — the figure in the mist
*See separate Kitsune document for full detail.*

Ancient — older than the shrine, older than the Inari tradition that frames it.

In this story it appears as:
- **The fox** — glimpsed occasionally in the forest near the circle; possibly present on the winter solstice morning as Shūmaru descends after leaving the poem. One line, perhaps less. He notes it. He keeps walking.
- **The figure in the mist** — the glimpse at the solstice, dancing kagura in the circle before dawn

Shūmaru finds the stone circle himself, by following the sound of water. He needs no magical assistance. His quality of attention is what makes everything possible. The fox, when glimpsed, confirms something — but does not lead, does not guide, does not explain.

**What the kitsune wants:** We will never know. The kami are incomprehensible to us. This is not a puzzle to be solved. It is the nature of the relationship between the human and the sacred.

### Tone and Aesthetic

- Quiet, interior, unhurried — a year in a boy's life, observed with care
- **Mono no aware** (物の哀れ) — the pathos of things, the beauty of impermanence — is the story's emotional key
- **Ma** (間 — the power of what is left unsaid), **wabi** (侘び — austere transient beauty), **sabi** (寂び — the beauty of age and impermanence)
- The supernatural is ambient, not dramatic. The glimpse is the story's one explicitly uncertain moment.
- Shūmaru's perspective is the only perspective we fully inhabit
- Mist as recurring motif — erasing boundaries, suspending the ordinary world
- The **ensō** — 円相 — as the story's deepest image: the circle, complete and not complete, open enough for something to have passed through

### The Year's Ritual Calendar
*To be developed — the richness of the boy's year is the story's surface texture.*

Key seasonal moments to research and develop:
- **New Year** (*Oshōgatsu*) — the most important festival of the year
- **Spring** — plum and cherry blossom seasons; the plum blossom is Ume's flower
- **Boys' Day** (*Tango no Sekku*, 5th day of 5th month)
- **The Great Summer Purification** (*Nagoshi no Harae*) — near the solstice; ritual cleansing at shrines
- **The summer solstice** (*Geshi*) — the glimpse
- **Autumn moon-viewing** (*Tsukimi*)
- **The winter solstice** (*Tōji*) — yuzu baths, red bean porridge, the year's turning; the poem
