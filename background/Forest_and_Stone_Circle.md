# The Forest and the Stone Circle
## Location Sheet — Shūmaru Project

---

## Overview

Above the shrine, beyond the estate's loosely defined boundary, the cryptomeria forest continues up the mountain slope. This is the part of Kiri no Ue that belongs to no one — or to something older than ownership. It is where Shūmaru is most himself, and where the story's central mystery lives.

The forest contains two significant locations: **the shrine**, which belongs to the family and is documented in the Estate sheet, and **the stone circle**, which belongs to no one and which Shūmaru found by following a fox.

---

## The Path Beyond the Shrine

From the shrine, no maintained path continues. But the forest is old enough that the ground between the cryptomeria trunks is relatively open — centuries of needle fall have suppressed the undergrowth. Walking is possible, quiet, the feet cushioned by accumulated duff.

The slope rises gently at first, then more steeply. The trees here are older than those near the shrine — their trunks wider, the canopy higher. Light arrives in columns and shifts with the time of day.

A faint sound becomes audible before long: the stream, running somewhere above. Following it — following the sound rather than any visible path — leads eventually to the clearing.

The walk from the shrine takes perhaps ten minutes. It feels longer.

---

## The Clearing

Where an old cryptomeria fell long ago — decades, perhaps centuries before Shūmaru's time — a gap opened in the canopy. The fallen trunk has mostly returned to the earth, a long mound of moss and fern, barely recognizable as a tree. In its place, low plants have colonized the gap: grasses, ferns, a few small deciduous saplings that have not yet grown tall enough to close the opening.

The result is a space of perhaps eight or ten meters across where sky is visible. Light comes through differently here — more directly, less filtered. In autumn the low sun reaches the clearing floor in a way it cannot penetrate the closed forest, casting that particular amber-gold of the season.

The stream runs along the clearing's northern edge, cold and clear, fast over stones in most places but slowing into a shallow pool where the rock formation interrupts its course.

**The quality of the clearing:** It has the feeling of a room. Not enclosed, but defined. The surrounding cryptomeria stand like walls that have agreed to leave this space open. Arriving in it from the forest feels like arriving somewhere that has been waiting.

---

## The Rock Formation

At the stream's edge, where the water slows into its pool: an outcrop of **columnar dacite** — volcanic rock that forms naturally in precise hexagonal columns, as if cut by a deliberate hand. Dark grey, almost black when wet from the stream's spray. The columns rise to perhaps two meters at their highest point, then break and tumble toward the water in a natural staircase of geometric forms.

This rock type occurs naturally in the mountains of Nagano and Niigata prefectures — it is geology, not human work. But it looks worked. It looks intentional. To a boy of Shūmaru's perceptive nature, standing before it for the first time, the distinction between natural and made feels suddenly uncertain.

Moss fills the crevices between columns. In wet seasons the whole formation is furred with it. In winter, ice forms in the cracks and the columns ring faintly when the temperature drops.

---

## The Stone Circle

At the base of the dacite outcrop, and extending in a partial arc toward the clearing's center: a remnant of something human and very old.

Small stones, none larger than a man's two fists, arranged in what was once a circle — perhaps four meters across. Most are now half-buried, tipped, pulled by root and frost over centuries. Moss covers them so thoroughly that they could be mistaken for natural features of the ground. You would walk past them without seeing them.

Shūmaru saw them on his first visit. He is not sure why he looked down at the right moment. He simply did.

**What they are:** A remnant of the **Jōmon period** — the ancient culture that preceded Japanese civilization by thousands of years. Jōmon people built stone circles throughout the Japanese archipelago, used for ritual, burial, and astronomical ceremony. The great circles of northern Japan are famous; this one is not famous, not documented, not known to anyone currently living.

In the Edo period, Shūmaru would have no word for Jōmon, no framework for understanding what he is looking at. He knows only that the stones are old — far older than the shrine, older perhaps than anything he has a name for — and that they were placed by human hands with purpose.

**Gen'nosuke's dead teacher's notes** contain a reference — imprecise, drawn from local tradition rather than direct observation — to a site of the old people somewhere on the mountain above the Kiriyama shrine. The teacher believed it connected to the spirit of the place, perhaps predating the Inari framework entirely. Gen'nosuke has looked for it. He has been looking from the wrong direction — approaching from the shrine rather than following the stream.

---

## Shūmaru's Stone

One stone of the circle is flat, wide, and slightly elevated — tipped by centuries of frost until it presents a natural seat at the stream's edge. From it, the pool is directly below, reflecting sky and the dark geometry of the columnar dacite.

This is Shūmaru's stone. He found it on his first visit and has returned to it on every subsequent one. He does not think of it as his — that would be the wrong kind of claim. But it receives him. It is the right size for a slight boy. His feet can almost reach the water.

He has sat here in every season:
- **Spring** — snowmelt makes the stream loud and fast, the pool turbulent and silty
- **Summer** — the water clears, insects hover over the pool's surface, the clearing is warm
- **Autumn** — his season. The amber light. The maples at the clearing's edge turned. The mist in the valley below at morning
- **Winter** — the stream runs under ice at its edges, the pool reduced to a dark moving lens. His footprints in the snow are the only ones

## The *Shinki* — 神気

From the first visit, Shūmaru has felt the *shinki* here — divine presence, sacred atmosphere. Not the same quality as the shrine's *shinki*, which is watchful, protective, oriented toward the household below.

This is older. Less directed. It does not watch over anything in particular. It simply *is* — the way the mountain is, the way very old things are present without needing to announce themselves.

He does not try to analyze it. He would not know how. He simply knows that this place has *kehai* — presence — that is never absent, that was there on the first visit and is there on every visit, that does not diminish with familiarity.

Occasionally, at the edge of vision, something moves between the stones. A shape, low to the ground, quick. By the time he looks directly — nothing. He has learned not to look directly.

---

## Shūmaru's Discovery

On the spring equinox morning, going further into the forest than he has gone before, Shūmaru follows the sound of the stream. He does not know the stream is there — he simply hears it, above him and to the left, and goes toward it. This is how he moves through the world: following what is present toward its source.

He finds the clearing, and the stone circle, entirely by himself.

He needs no guide. His quality of attention is the thing that is unusual. The place was always there. He simply had not yet followed the water far enough to find it.

He returns repeatedly through spring and into summer, telling no one. It is his most private place — more private even than the shrine, which belongs to the family. The circle belongs to no one, which means it can belong to him in a way nothing else does.

**The fox** — Japanese red foxes (*Vulpes vulpes japonica*) inhabit this mountain forest. Shūmaru has seen them — glimpsed, at the edge of vision, never clearly. Occasionally near the circle. Whether the fox he sometimes senses at the clearing's edge is always the same animal, always a natural animal, he cannot say. He does not pursue the question. He notes it, as he notes everything, and leaves it unresolved.

On the winter solstice morning, descending through the cryptomeria after leaving the poem, he may glimpse a fox at the forest's edge. He does not stop. He does not look back. He notes it. He keeps walking.

---

## The First Sight of Yukino

On a morning in autumn — mist heavy in the valley, the amber light just beginning in the clearing — Shūmaru arrives at the tree line and stops.

There is a figure among the stones.

A girl, slight, perhaps his age, performing what he will later understand to be **神楽 *kagura*** — sacred Shinto dance, ancient in form, the body becoming a vessel for something beyond itself. Slow in places, suddenly quick in others. The arms particularly expressive, the gestures carrying meaning he cannot read but feels the weight of.

She dances for the stones. For whatever inhabits them. Not for him — he is certain of this, standing at the tree line, completely still in the way that costs him nothing.

He watches through the mist. The figure is not quite clear — the mist does what mist does, dissolves edges, makes certain and uncertain the same thing. But she is unmistakably there. Unmistakably real, or something adjacent to real that he has no better word for.

The dance ends.

She is still.

Then — without turning — she speaks to him.

She knew he was there. She was not surprised. His arrival was not an intrusion.

**What she says is not yet determined.** It will be the first words she ever speaks to him. They will matter.

## What This Place Means for the Story

The stone circle is the story's deepest location — deeper even than the shrine. It is where:

- Shūmaru is most himself, most private, most free
- The supernatural is most present and least explicable
- Yukino first appears, and where they will meet repeatedly
- The mystery Gen'nosuke has been pursuing from the scholarly direction already lives, waiting to be found

The shrine is the family's relationship with the spirit world — sanctioned, named, maintained by ritual. The stone circle is something prior to all of that. Something that existed before the family, before the shrine, before the name Inari was given to whatever dwells here.

Whether Yukino is the spirit of the shrine come to a different place, or the spirit of the stones, or something else entirely — this is one of the story's questions that will not be answered.
