## Sleeping Arrangements

### The Bedding
A high-ranking samurai household in the Edo period slept on a **futon** — but not in the modern Western sense. The traditional sleeping arrangement consisted of two elements:

**敷き布団 *Shikibuton*** — the mattress. A cotton-stuffed pad laid directly on the tatami floor. For a household of the Kiriyama's rank, this would be of good quality — thick cotton batting enclosed in silk or fine cotton fabric. It is firm rather than soft. The floor's proximity is a feature, not a deficiency — it is simply how the world is arranged.

**夜着 *Yogi*** — the sleeping covering. This is the distinctive and important detail. The *yogi* is not a blanket — it is a large, kimono-shaped garment stuffed with cotton batting. The sleeper covers himself with it as if it were a vast, soft kimono laid over the body. The sleeves extend on either side of the sleeping form — not worn through, simply present, like wings laid open. For a boy sleeping alone in a mountain estate in early spring, the *yogi* would be thick and warm.

The *yogi*'s kimono shape is not accidental — it descends from an era when a samurai needed to be able to rise instantly and be dressed for battle. By Shūmaru's time, this practical origin has dissolved into simply the way things are done. He has always slept beneath a *yogi*. He has never questioned its shape.

**枕 *Makura*** — the pillow. Traditional Japanese pillows are firm, filled with **蕎麦殻 *sobagara*** (buckwheat chaff), which molds slightly to the shape of the head while remaining supportive. Considerably harder than any Western pillow. The head rests on it at a specific angle — not sunk into softness but cradled at the neck.

### The Room at Night
Shūmaru's futon is laid out each evening by a servant — he does not arrange it himself, this being a servant's task in a household of this rank. In the morning it will be rolled and stored.

The room is dark — no lamplight after he retires, unless he keeps a small oil lamp burning, which he sometimes does when he wants to read. The tatami breathes in a particular way at night, a dry, faintly grassy smell. In winter a small **火鉢 *hibachi*** (charcoal brazier) provides heat — inadequate against the mountain cold, but the only warmth. By spring the hibachi has been put away. The room is cold in the early morning.

His **shōji** screens face toward the trees. When there is wind, the cryptomeria are audible — a long, continuous sound like something breathing. In complete stillness, he can sometimes hear the distant stream above the shrine.

## Hair

### The Stages of a Boy's Hair
Hair in Edo period Japan was a precise social code, marking a person's age, status, and position exactly.

**Early childhood (before age 7–8):** A young child's hair was worn relatively freely — not yet cut in the formal adult-approaching styles.

**The wakashū stage (approximately ages 8–17):** A boy in this transitional period between childhood and the coming-of-age ceremony (*genpuku*) wore a distinctive hairstyle called **前髪 *maegami*** — literally "front hair." The crown of the head was partially shaved, and the remaining hair — particularly the **forelocks** at the front and sides — was left long and tied or folded back into a modest topknot. This partial shave typically began around age 11 or 12.

**若衆髷 *Wakashū mage*** — the young man's topknot. The top of the head is partially shaved, the remaining hair oiled and tied. The forelocks are the defining feature — they mark the boy as *wakashū*, in the transitional stage. They are combed back and secured; the loose ends may drape on either side of the topknot.

**The adult hairstyle — 丁髷 *Chonmage*:** At the *genpuku* coming-of-age ceremony (typically age 16–17 in this period), the forelocks were shaved off entirely, completing the fully shaved pate with topknot that is the iconic adult samurai appearance.

### Shūmaru's Hair Specifically
At the story's opening (age 8–9), Shūmaru has not yet had the formal partial shaving of the crown. His hair is in the transitional stage of late childhood — dark, straight, tied back loosely with a simple cord. Not yet the formal *wakashū* style, but moving toward it.

By the story's peak (age 10), the partial shaving may have begun — the crown showing the first marks of his transition, the forelocks becoming the defined feature of his appearance.

His hair is always neat — a household of this rank maintains its members' appearance carefully. But in the early morning, before the household's servant has attended to him, it would be partially loose from sleep.

**Camellia oil (椿油 *tsubaki abura*)** was the primary hair oil used to dress the topknot and keep the style in place. The smell is faint, clean, slightly floral. It is one of the smells of the house.

## Sleeping Clothing

### What He Wears to Sleep
The concept of dedicated sleepwear was not fully developed in the early Edo period. Shūmaru sleeps in a **寝間着 *nemaki*** — a simpler, lighter kimono used for sleeping. For a household of the Kiriyama's rank, this would be cotton rather than the silk of his formal wear — plain, in an unpatterned or simply patterned fabric.

Beneath the *nemaki*, an **襦袢 *juban*** — an undergarment worn against the skin, a layer of warmth and modesty.

He does not sleep in his day clothing — changing into and out of sleeping dress is part of the household's daily ritual.

## Morning Ritual

### The Sequence of Waking
Shūmaru wakes before anyone else. This is simply his nature — not discipline, not effort, simply what his body does. The sky outside his shōji is gray, becoming pale gold.

He lies still for a moment first. This is important to understand — he does not leap up. He lies beneath the *yogi* and listens. The quality of the silence. Whether there is wind in the cryptomeria. Whether he can hear the stream. Whether he can detect the faint smell of Gen'nosuke's incense from the corridor, which would mean the tutor has already risen.

Then he rises.

### Rising and Folding
He folds back the *yogi* — the large soft kimono-cover — and sits on the edge of his *shikibuton* mattress. The tatami is cold under his feet. In early spring the mountain cold is still present in the mornings, particularly in the first light.

In a household of this rank, the futon is rolled and stored by a servant — but this happens later, when the household is fully awake. In the early morning, before anyone else has risen, Shūmaru leaves it arranged. He will not be here when it is put away.

### Face Washing
Cold water, from a basin filled the previous evening or fetched fresh from the well. A **手ぬぐい *tenugui*** — a thin cotton towel — for drying. The cold water is not unpleasant to him. He has always done this. The shock of it is simply the beginning of the day.

**Oral hygiene:** A small twig used to clean the teeth, or salt applied with the finger. The taste of salt in the early morning is one of the day's small constants.

### Hair
In the early morning, he attends to his hair himself — roughly, briefly. The servant who properly dresses the household's hair works later in the morning, when the household is awake. Before that, Shūmaru ties his hair back simply with a cord. It is not the formal dressed style. It is the hair of a boy who is about to go somewhere before anyone notices.

### Dressing
Over the **juban** undergarment: his **kosode** — the kimono of daily informal wear. For a boy of his rank, silk, in an appropriate subdued color. Dark blue, perhaps, or a quiet grey-green. Tied at the waist with a simple **帯 *obi*** sash.

In colder months, an additional layer — a **羽織 *haori*** jacket over the kimono. In early spring, he judges whether he needs it. He often does. The mountain mornings are cold even after the snow has gone.

**下駄 *Geta*** — wooden sandals raised on two teeth, worn outdoors over **足袋 *tabi*** socks (split-toed, white). Or for moving quietly around the estate's corridors and veranda, simply tabi-clad feet.

He dresses quickly and quietly. He is practiced at not making sound.

### Before the Household Wakes
He slides open his shōji screen — carefully, the wooden frame requiring a specific pressure to move silently. The corridor is dark, the polished wood floor cold through his tabi socks.

If Gen'nosuke's incense is already present — that faint thread of sandalwood or *jinkō* — he pauses for a moment. Noting it. Not deciding anything. Just noting.

Then he moves toward the veranda and the garden beyond. The mist is in the valley. The day has not yet claimed him.

## Clothing for the Day

### Informal Household Wear
**小袖 *Kosode*** — the basic kimono. For boys of wealthy samurai families, silk in appropriate colors. More subdued than children of merchants or commoners — the samurai aesthetic favors restraint. Dark blue (*kon*), deep grey (*nezumi*), muted green. The fabric is patterned subtly if at all — small repeated designs, or plain.

**帯 *Obi*** — the sash that ties the kimono at the waist. For informal daily wear, a simple version, not the elaborate adult formal style.

### Formal and Lesson Wear
**袴 *Hakama*** — wide, pleated trousers worn over the kimono for formal occasions, lessons, and martial training. For a boy of Shūmaru's age and class, this is the clothing of the lesson room and the practice hall. The **袴着 *hakamagi*** ceremony — wearing hakama for the first time — was reserved for aristocratic and warrior families and occurred between ages ten and twelve. Shūmaru is at or just past this ceremony.

**裃 *Kamishimo*** — the more formal combination of kataginu (a sleeveless overvest with pronounced shoulders) and hakama, worn for official or formal occasions. This is adult formal wear; Shūmaru would not yet wear the full version.

### Outdoor and Travel Wear
**羽織 *Haori*** — a hip-length jacket worn over the kimono, for warmth and for going outdoors or receiving guests informally.

**雨具 *Amagu*** — for rain: a straw raincoat (*kappa*) and oiled paper umbrella.

**草鞋 *Waraji*** — straw sandals, for more extended walking outdoors. Or **下駄 *geta*** — the raised wooden sandals for shorter distances.
