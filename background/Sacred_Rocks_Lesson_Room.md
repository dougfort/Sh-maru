## Part One: The Shinto Significance of the Stone Circle

This is the most important section for the story — particularly for Gen'nosuke's explanation to Shūmaru of what he has found.

### 岩倉 Iwakura — Sacred Rocks

*Iwakura* (岩倉) refers to the belief in rocks as *yorishiro* containing kami in ancient Shinto. It also refers to the rock itself, which is the object of worship. Nature worship — also known as spirit worship or animism — is a type of base faith that has been present in Japan since ancient times.

*Iwakura*, in Shinto tradition, refers to sacred rock formations revered as sites where kami — the divine spirits or deities — are invited to descend and temporarily reside for the purpose of worship. These rocks function as *yorishiro*, temporary dwelling places for the kami, and are integral to a broader rock-worshipping cult that includes related concepts such as *ishigami* (rock deities) and *iwasaka* (sacred enclosures around rocks). The veneration of iwakura traces its origins to prehistoric Japan, with archaeological evidence from the Jōmon period.

The Shintō that we know today can be traced directly to worship of *kannabi*, *iwakura*, *himorogi*, and *hi* — the indigenous faith of the Japanese hunter-gatherers of the Jōmon era. The sites of shrines were the same sacred mountains, forests, and rock formations where the Japanese had worshipped for centuries.

This is the crucial point for Gen'nosuke's scholarship: **the Kiriyama shrine was almost certainly built near the stone circle because the stone circle was already sacred**. The shrine is the later, named, institutionalized form of worship at a site that was sacred long before it had a name. The Inari association came later still.

### 磐境 Iwasaka — The Sacred Enclosure

An *iwasaka* (磐境) is a stone altar or mound erected as a *yorishiro* to call a kami for worship. A *banjiki*, on the other hand, is similar to a stone circle — an ancient ruin made of stones arranged in a ring — and serves as a boundary that marks the divine realm and preserves the sacredness of the site.

The stone circle Shūmaru has found is, in Shinto terms, an *iwasaka* or *banjiki* — a sacred boundary enclosure around the columnar dacite rock formation, which is itself an *iwakura*. The circle marks where the divine realm begins.

### The Rock Formation as Yorishiro

Found in forest clearings and other natural settings, *iwakura* were cordoned off with rice-fiber ropes, and the ground around them strewn with pebbles, in what could well be a seminal model of the dry landscape garden.

Kami do not have physical bodies. They are like energy or radio waves floating in the air. To interact with them, they need an antenna or a vessel to descend into. This vessel is called a *yorishiro*. *Iwakura* (Sacred Rock): Massive boulders are believed to be solid enough to hold a god's weight.

The columnar dacite formation at Shūmaru's clearing is, in these terms, a natural *yorishiro* of the most powerful kind — an unusual, geometrically striking rock that has drawn kami presence for thousands of years. The Jōmon circle of stones around it is the enclosure that marks and protects this.

### The Solar Connection

One of the most compelling pieces of evidence for the "calendar theory" can be found at the Oyu Stone Circles in Akita Prefecture. A line connecting the centers of the Nonakado and Manza circles, passing through their respective sun-dial-shaped stone clusters, aligns perfectly with the direction of the sunset on the summer solstice.

This is the detail that will stop Gen'nosuke in his tracks. Many Jōmon stone circles were oriented to the summer solstice. The circle Shūmaru has found may be oriented the same way — which would mean the solstice morning, when Shūmaru witnesses the figure dancing, is not coincidental. The circle was built to mark exactly that moment. The midsummer dawn at the stone circle is the moment the site was designed for.

When Gen'nosuke understands this — when he connects the circle's probable orientation to the solstice, and Shūmaru's presence there on that particular morning — something will shift in him. Not scholarly excitement. Something quieter and more serious.

### The Jōmon Spirit World

To the Jōmon, each object, animate and inanimate, housed a spirit. Throwing things away would have been done ceremonially.

For hunter-gatherers to expend such extraordinary effort and time and organize themselves in such activity, it had to be targeted at supreme or supernatural beings. We also now have the benefit of archaeological comparisons with Altaian stone circles which are so remarkably similar, as are their mythology and rites and animistic beliefs.

The Jōmon worldview is the deepest substrate of Japanese spirituality — older than Shinto as a formal tradition, older than Buddhism's arrival, older than the Chinese influence that shaped the educated world Gen'nosuke inhabits. Buddhism, with its elaborate temples and sacred images, was completely alien to the Japanese until it was imported from the Korean Peninsula in the sixth century. Not coincidentally, Shintō shrines also began springing up in the sixth century. But the sites of these shrines were the same sacred mountains, forests, and rock formations where the Japanese had worshipped for centuries.

Gen'nosuke's heterodox scholarship — his Kokugaku interest, his investigation of what existed before the named traditions — is precisely the framework for understanding this. He has been trying to find what is oldest at Kiri no Ue. The stone circle is the answer.

### What Gen'nosuke Can Tell Shūmaru

When Shūmaru shares the stone circle with Gen'nosuke, and Gen'nosuke eventually visits it, the tutor can offer Shūmaru a framework built from these concepts:

**岩倉 *Iwakura*** — the rock formation itself, a natural vessel for kami presence. Unusual geology draws kami. The columnar dacite, geometrically precise, anciently formed, would be recognized immediately by anyone with Gen'nosuke's knowledge as a significant *iwakura*.

**磐境 *Iwasaka*** — the stone circle around it, the sacred enclosure. Ancient people built this boundary to mark where the divine realm began. They knew the rock was special long before any shrine existed, long before Inari's name was given to anything.

**The solar alignment** — the circle may face the summer solstice sunrise. This means the site was designed for exactly the moment Shūmaru was there when he saw what he saw.

**The continuity** — the Kiriyama shrine below is the named, institutionalized descendant of whatever was worshipped at this circle. The kami at the shrine and the kami at the circle are connected — or may be the same, in different layers of human naming.

**What this means for Shūmaru** — he found the oldest layer. Below the Inari name, below the shrine, below whatever named tradition the family maintains, there is something that has been here since the Jōmon people built their circle of stones. He found it by following the sound of water, on an ordinary spring morning, because that is simply how he moves through the world.

Gen'nosuke will need to sit with this for some time before he speaks of it.

## Part Two: The Lesson Room

### Physical Arrangement

A private tutor's lesson room in a samurai household would be a **tatami-floored room**, typically modest in size — perhaps six to eight mats. It would contain:

**Low writing desks** (*bunzukue* or *fudemaki*) — for both teacher and student. These are very low, perhaps 30cm high, used while kneeling on the tatami. The desk surface is bare wood, smooth and clean, with a slight lip at the front to prevent brushes rolling off.

**The Four Treasures of the Study** (*文房四宝 bunbō-shihō*) — the essential calligraphy tools, present on every desk:
- **筆 *Fude*** — the brush. Bamboo handle, animal hair tip (goat, horse, or weasel). Multiple sizes — a thick brush for large characters, a fine brush for smaller work. The brush is held vertically, perpendicular to the paper, gripped between thumb and first two fingers, the whole arm moving rather than just the wrist.
- **墨 *Sumi*** — the ink stick. Solid compressed ink, made from soot and animal glue (*nikawa*), formed into a stick. To use solid ink, you add water little by little to an inkstone and grind it by hand to make ink. The grinding is slow, meditative, preparation for the work ahead. The smell of fresh ink — particular, slightly alkaline, unmistakable — rises as it is ground.
- **硯 *Suzuri*** — the inkstone. Slate or fine-grained stone, with a flat grinding surface and a small well to collect the liquid ink. Each student has their own. Shūmaru's inkstone would be of good quality — his family's means expressed even in his equipment.
- **紙 *Kami*** — the paper. **和紙 *Washi*** — traditional Japanese paper, handmade from plant fibers, slightly absorbent, thin. Each stroke is permanent — you cannot erase or fix mistakes. This irreversibility is what gives shodo its tension and requiring mindfulness.

**Additional tools:**
- **文鎮 *Bunchin*** — a paperweight, to keep the washi from shifting
- **下敷き *Shitajiki*** — a soft underlay mat beneath the paper, for cushioning
- **筆架 *Fukkake*** — a brush rest, to lay the brush on between strokes

**Texts:** The lesson room would have scrolls or bound texts of the Confucian classics — the *Analects*, the *Great Learning*, the *Doctrine of the Mean* — and Japanese literary texts. Gen'nosuke's own books would be stacked carefully, some Chinese, some Japanese, some in scripts Shūmaru cannot yet fully read.

**The room's character:** Students learned by copying *tehon* (model texts) repeatedly with brush and ink, absorbing not only the techniques of writing but also proper posture, brush handling, and a calm mental attitude. Gen'nosuke's lesson room has this quality — it is a room where attention is the purpose. The smell of ink and old paper. The small sound of brush on washi. Long silences that are not empty.

### How Gen'nosuke Teaches

The proverb "Your writing shows who you are" reflected the belief that handwriting revealed a person's disposition and inner state. Teachers didn't merely instruct students on character forms — they also taught proper sitting posture, manners, and how to care for writing tools, emphasizing the inseparability of daily life and calligraphy. Writing became a form of self-cultivation, a practice in courtesy, and a method of attuning one's spirit.

For Gen'nosuke, calligraphy is particularly resonant — his Zen practice shares the same underlying philosophy. In Zen Buddhism, an ensō is a circle that is hand-drawn in one or two uninhibited brushstrokes to express a moment when the mind is free to let the body create. The ensō on Gen'nosuke's wall above his altar was brushed in exactly this way — a single stroke, the mind emptied, the arm moving from the shoulder.

When he watches Shūmaru grind ink — the slow circular motion, the meditative quality of the preparation — he notices that the boy is already doing something Zen practitioners spend years trying to achieve: he is simply present to the action, without anticipation or impatience.

### The Lesson's Rhythm

A typical lesson morning with Gen'nosuke:

1. **Arrival and preparation** — Shūmaru enters, bows, takes his place at the writing desk. He grinds his own ink — this is not skipped, not delegated. The grinding is the beginning of attention.

2. **Reading aloud** — a passage from the assigned text, first Gen'nosuke reading it, then Shūmaru repeating it, then Shūmaru reading independently. The Confucian texts are read in classical Chinese (*kanbun*), which requires separate study of the reading conventions.

3. **Calligraphy** — copying assigned characters or passages. Gen'nosuke sets the *tehon* (model text) and watches Shūmaru work. He corrects posture, brush angle, the placement of strokes. He rarely praises. He occasionally says: *again*.

4. **Discussion** — sometimes, not always. Gen'nosuke asks a question about the passage. Not always a question with a clear answer. Sometimes a question that is meant to open something rather than close it.

5. **The kōan** — occasionally, without announcement, a kōan offered into the silence. Shūmaru receives these in his particular way — going quiet and inward. Gen'nosuke watches.

6. **Silence** — long periods when both are simply reading or writing. The lesson room is comfortable with silence in a way few rooms are.

## Part Three: Horses and the Stables

The stables and Shūmaru's riding lessons have been documented in the Estate sheet. Some additional detail for the writing:

**The horses' smell** — hay, horse sweat, leather, and the particular warm animal smell that is different from all other smells. The stables are warm even in the mountain winters.

**Bajutsu for a boy his age** — at eight or nine, he is learning the basics: how to approach a horse, how to mount without assistance (difficult for a slight boy with a large animal), how to sit correctly — weight balanced, heels down, hands light. The head groom teaches this with patience and directness. He does not use the ceremonial language of the lesson room. He says: *heels down. Sit back. Let your weight fall.*

**Shūmaru and the horse** — he has a particular horse. Not the most impressive horse in the stable — a mare of moderate size, dark bay, with a calm temperament. She responds to stillness. Nervous riders make her nervous; Shūmaru's natural quiet settles her immediately. The head groom noticed this early and said nothing about it directly — simply assigned her to the boy.

**The groom's boy** — a child roughly Shūmaru's age, perhaps a year older, who works in the stables. His name is not yet determined. He and Shūmaru have exchanged very few words. But Shūmaru has observed him carefully — the easy way he moves among the horses, the knowledge in his hands, the world he inhabits which has nothing to do with Confucian texts or calligraphy. He is not educated in any way Gen'nosuke would recognize. He knows everything about horses.
