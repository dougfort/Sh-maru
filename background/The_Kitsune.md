# The Kitsune
## Character Document — Shūmaru Project

---

## Nature

The being that inhabits the Kiriyama mountain is a single ancient consciousness that moves between forms the way water moves between states — ice, water, mist — always the same substance. It is not three entities sharing information. It is one awareness that has always known everything experienced by any of its forms, and a great deal more besides.

It is older than the shrine. Older than the Inari tradition that now frames it. Older than the Kiriyama family, older than any name currently given to what it is. The stone circle in the forest above is not its home exactly — it has no home in the human sense — but it is the place on this mountain where its presence is most concentrated, most itself.

Whether it has a true name is uncertain. If it does, that name is not Japanese — it predates Japan. It may predate language. It may be something felt rather than spoken: the particular quality of stillness at the stone circle, the *shinki* that Shūmaru has always felt there. That sensation may be the closest thing to its name that human perception can access.

**Yukino** and **Tsuta** are names it has offered to the human world — one for each face it turns toward us. Neither is its name. Both are true, in the way that *mist* and *water* are both true names for the same substance in different conditions.

## The Three Forms

### 狐 The Fox
The form without personality, without name, without the performance of being anything for anyone. When it is the fox it is simply what it is — an animal moving through the forest on its own business, following its own purposes, which are not human purposes.

**Appearance:** A Japanese red fox (*Vulpes vulpes japonica*) — reddish-brown, lean, amber-eyed. Indistinguishable from any natural fox of these mountain forests. This is deliberate. The form offers no supernatural signals, no uncanny quality. Only the behavior is occasionally odd — pausing to look back, moving with particular intention, appearing and disappearing without the usual sounds of an animal in undergrowth.

**What it knows:** Everything. When it is the fox it is still the full consciousness, present in animal form. The fox that led Shūmaru to the stone circle was not unaware of what it was doing. The fox that Shūmaru sometimes glimpses between the stones is watching him with full knowledge of who he is and what has passed between them in other forms.

**What Shūmaru senses:** That this fox — or these foxes, he cannot always be sure — is somehow attended. That being seen by it is different from being seen by an ordinary animal. He has never been able to say exactly why he feels this. He has not tried very hard to say it.

---

### 雪乃 Yukino — the young woman
*See also: Yukino entry in Story Bible*

The face turned toward Shūmaru specifically. Young, approximately his age in apparent years — though what age means for something this old is a question the story does not answer. Playful, elusive, aristocratic in bearing and education without any traceable family to account for it.

**Why this form, why this age:** Uncertain. Perhaps she meets each person at the age most useful for what passes between them. Perhaps something about Shūmaru required a companion, not a superior or a teacher. Perhaps she simply chose it, the way very old things sometimes make choices that have no explanation in human terms.

**What she knows when she is Yukino:** Everything — including that Shūmaru followed the fox to the clearing, including every morning he has sat on his stone before she appeared, including every thought he has turned over privately about her nature. She was there for all of it. She simply has not said so.

**The kagura dance:** She was performing an offering to whatever inhabits the stones — which is herself, which she knows. This is not paradox in her experience, though it would be in human experience. The dance is genuine. The offering is genuine. The being can make offerings to itself because it contains the stone circle's spirit the way a river contains the water that flows through it.

**Her playfulness:** Real, not performed. Something about Shūmaru — his particular quality of attention, his willingness to sit with uncertainty without grasping at resolution — produces in her something that in a human being would be called delight. She enjoys his company in a way she has not enjoyed anything for a very long time. This may be the truest thing about their relationship.

**The tell Shūmaru never quite sees:** When she is very still — listening to something he cannot hear — her eyes for a moment hold a quality that is not human. Not frightening. Simply ancient. He has glimpsed this twice. Both times he looked away before he could be certain of what he saw.

### 蔦 Tsuta — the old woman
The face turned toward the human world's edge. Old — genuinely old in appearance, perhaps seventy or more. She has been coming to the Kiriyama shrine since before anyone currently living can remember her not coming.

**Name:** 蔦 *Tsuta* — ivy. Something that persists, that clings to old stone, that is woven into the fabric of ancient places. A name offered to the village, to the gardener, to the human world she moves through in this form. Not her name.

**Appearance:** Small, very thin, moving with the careful economy of great age. White hair, simply dressed in the manner of a village woman of no particular means. Her face has the quality of very old wood — the grain showing through everything, nothing superfluous remaining. Her eyes are the most notable thing about her: dark, steady, giving the impression of looking at something just behind whatever is in front of her.

**What she is in the village's understanding:** Not a priest — she has no official standing. In the older tradition she might have been called a *miko* in the original shamanic sense, a woman who speaks for the spirits. By the Edo period this is officially suppressed. She is simply known, locally, as someone who has a relationship with the old places. People come to her quietly, for things they cannot bring to the official shrine — questions about the dead, about the kami, about why a particular place feels the way it feels.

**Her relationship with the Kiriyama shrine:** She has been coming for generations. She brings simple offerings — rice, sake, a seasonal branch. She tidies. She sits for a while. She leaves. The gardener has always known about her and leaves the gate unlatched on certain mornings. Neither has ever discussed this arrangement directly. It simply exists.

**What she knows about the stone circle:** In this form, she knows everything — she is the stone circle, in the same way she is the shrine and the fox. But Tsuta does not go to the stone circle. That is not this form's territory. The human world's edge is as far as Tsuta goes. The stone circle is beyond the edge.

**Her relationship with Shūmaru:** She has watched him since before he could walk the path alone. She remembers his grandfather. In this form she has never spoken to him directly — he has only glimpsed her at the shrine, or heard the gardener's oblique references. But she has watched him with the full knowledge of what passes between them at the stone circle.

If Shūmaru ever spoke to her directly — if he ever found the courage to approach her at the shrine and ask her name — she would tell him *Tsuta*. And watch his face carefully. To see if he is ready for what that name, in proximity to the other name he knows, might suggest.

## What the Being Wants

If *want* is even the right word for what something this ancient experiences.

It has protected this mountain for longer than the Kiriyama family has existed. It has watched families come and go, children grow and age, the forest change slowly over centuries. It has never before revealed itself to any child of the household — not as Yukino, not as Tsuta in direct conversation, not as the fox with unmistakable intention.

Something about Shūmaru is different. His quality of perception — the fact that he notices things without grasping at them, that he can sit with uncertainty without needing to resolve it — may be exactly the quality required to receive an encounter with something this old without being harmed by it. He approaches **無心 *mushin*** — no-mind, pure awareness — instinctively, as a matter of nature. This is what Zen practitioners spend lifetimes trying to achieve. He simply is it, some of the time, without knowing it has a name.

She may have been waiting for him. Or waiting for someone like him. For a very long time.

What she wants from the encounter — if the word applies — may not translate into human intention at all. It may be something closer to: *recognition*. To be seen, once, by a human being who can bear the full weight of what she is, without flinching and without grasping.

Whether Shūmaru is that person — whether he can grow into it by the story's end — is the story's deepest question.

## What Gen'nosuke Will Eventually Understand

Gen'nosuke's scholarship gives him the framework: kitsune of great age can take multiple forms. His dead teacher's notes contain references to the Kiriyama spirit being older than its Inari designation. The tradition of *shinbutsu-shūgō* — the blending of Shinto and Buddhist thought — gives him language for a spirit that predates its own names.

When Shūmaru tells him, in small careful pieces, about the fox, the clearing, the stones, the girl — Gen'nosuke will begin to assemble something. And when he asks, quietly, whether Shūmaru has seen anyone else near the shrine — an older woman perhaps — and Shūmaru goes still —

Gen'nosuke will understand that he is dealing with a three-form being of very great age. That the Yukino and the Tsuta and the fox are one. That his dead teacher was right. That the stone circle is real and Shūmaru has been sitting at its center for months.

**What he does with this understanding is the story's crucial question.** The scholar in him will want to document, investigate, complete his teacher's work. But what Shūmaru has with this being is personal, fragile, and entirely outside the scholarly framework. If Gen'nosuke approaches it as a scholar — if he tries to go to the stone circle, to see Yukino, to verify — something will be damaged or lost.

Whether he understands this in time — whether the scholar can step back far enough — is one of the story's genuine tensions, and perhaps its greatest test of Gen'nosuke's character.
