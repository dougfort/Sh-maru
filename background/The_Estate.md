# The Estate
## Location and Setting Sheet — Shūmaru Project

---

## Name
**Estate name:** 霧の上 *Kiri no Ue* — "above the mist"
**Family name:** 霧山 *Kiriyama* — *mist* + *mountain*

The estate name and family name share the character 霧 (*kiri*, mist) — not by design exactly, but naturally, the way a family and their place become the same thing over generations.

---

## Location
**Region:** The mountain country of central Honshū — the old province of Shinano (modern Nagano prefecture) or the border country between Shinano and Kai. Remote, ancient in character, deeply forested.

**Situation:** The estate sits on a raised slope or shoulder of mountain, looking down into a valley. Behind it, forested mountain rises steeply. Below, a river valley — farming villages, rice paddies — largely invisible in morning mist.

**Nearest settlement:** A small castle town or village at some distance. Close enough to reach in a day's travel; far enough to feel genuinely remote.

---

## The Landscape Character

The dominant experience of this landscape:

- **Mist** — the valley below fills with it most mornings, erasing the lower world. The estate seems to float above it.
- **Forest** — old cryptomeria (*sugi*) on the slopes behind. Dark, tall, the trunks straight and massive. The light inside the forest is different from the light outside it.
- **Seasonal extremes** — cold winters with heavy snow. Springs of sudden, violent beauty. Long contemplative autumns. This is Shūmaru's season — *shū*, autumn.
- **Silence** — broken by wind in the cryptomeria, water, birds. The silence itself has texture.

---

## The Places of the Estate

### 1. The Main House (*Omoya*)
The center of formal life. Low, dark-timbered, with deep overhanging eaves designed for heavy mountain snow. 

- Tatami-floored rooms, sliding paper screens (*shōji*) and heavier wooden screens (*fusuma*)
- Formal reception rooms face the garden — these are adult spaces, ceremonial spaces
- Interior corridors of polished dark wood, cold underfoot in winter
- The smell of tatami, cedar, incense, and woodsmoke
- Decorated with painted screens (*byōbu*) — likely landscapes, birds, seasonal imagery in the Rinpa style
- A small room that serves as a study and library — Confucian texts, poetry collections, perhaps a copy of Bashō

**Shūmaru's relationship to the house:** He moves through it correctly, performs its rituals correctly. But it is not where he is most himself.

---

### 1a. Shūmaru's Room
At a corner of the house — not the formal front, which faces the garden, but a side position that faces toward the path and the trees. From here the cryptomeria are visible, and at night their sound enters the room when the wind moves through them.

- Eight tatami mats — comfortable but not extravagant
- His *futon* laid on the tatami at night, rolled and stored during the day
- A low writing desk, positioned to catch morning light
- A small cabinet for his belongings — his inkstone, his brushes, a few objects he has gathered from the estate grounds: a smooth stone, a cryptomeria cone, things that caught his attention
- *Shōji* screens on two sides — one facing the corner of the garden, one facing the trees
- In winter, a small *hibachi* (charcoal brazier) — inadequate against the mountain cold, but the only warmth

**At night:** The household goes quiet around him. He lies on his futon and listens. The cryptomeria when there is wind. The particular silence of snowfall. Occasionally, and only when the wind is exactly right, the sound of the stream above the shrine — just audible, then gone. He is not frightened of the dark. He is attentive to it.

**In the early morning:** He wakes before the household stirs — before servants, before his tutor. The light through the shōji is gray, becoming pale gold. The mist is at its deepest in the valley. This is his freest time. This is almost certainly when he goes to the shrine.

---

### 2. The Garden
Between the house and the edge. Carefully designed, maintained by a gardener.

- A pond, very still, reflecting sky and the shapes of pines
- Shaped pines (*matsu*) — trained over years into deliberate forms
- Stepping stones crossing the pond
- Raked gravel suggesting water or emptiness
- A stone lantern (*tōrō*), lit on certain evenings
- At the garden's far boundary, the order begins to loosen — moss replaces gravel, the ground becomes uneven

**The garden's quality:** Contemplative, beautiful, slightly melancholy. Designed to be viewed from the house as much as walked through. Shūmaru sits at the edge of the veranda and looks at it.

---

### 3. The Edge
The threshold between the cultivated and the wild. Not dramatic — easy to miss if you are not paying attention.

- Where raked gravel gives way to moss and root
- Perhaps a low stone boundary, worn and old
- The ground here is slightly damp even in dry weather
- The trees begin here — at first spaced and light, then closing

**The edge's quality:** This is where Shūmaru pauses before going further. He has crossed it many times. It still requires a small, internal decision each time.

---

### 4. The Path
Through the trees, toward the shrine. Not long in distance — perhaps two or three minutes of walking — but it feels longer.

- Old cryptomeria on both sides, their trunks wider than a man's armspan
- The path is worn but not maintained — roots cross it, moss covers the stones
- Light filters down in columns, changes quality with season and time of day
- In autumn, cedar needles on the ground. In winter, snow held in the branches above, occasionally falling in sudden soft masses.
- The path bends once, so the shrine is not visible from the edge — it reveals itself.

**The path's quality:** Transitional. The world of the house recedes here. Something else becomes possible.

---

### 5. The Shrine
Small. Older than the house, older than the current family's memory. The family has maintained it for as long as anyone can say, but they did not build it.

- A **torii gate** — vermillion, but the paint is old and weathered in places, the wood darkened beneath
- Two **stone foxes** (*kitsune*) flanking the entrance — their features softened by weather and moss. One sits slightly higher than the other.
- A small **offering box** (*saisen-bako*) of darkened wood
- Behind the gate, a very small sanctuary building (*honden*) — barely large enough to stand inside. Its doors are kept closed.
- **Rope** (*shimenawa*) hung across the gate — thick, twisted rice straw, marking sacred space
- An old **cryptomeria** grows immediately beside the shrine, its roots lifting the ground around it. This tree is very old — older than the shrine itself, perhaps.

**The shrine's quality:** Absolute stillness. Even wind seems to pause here. Shūmaru has always felt that someone is present, though he has never seen anyone. This feeling is not frightening to him. It is simply a fact about the place.

---

### 6. Beyond the Shrine
The estate's boundary is loosely defined here. The forest continues up the mountain slope. There is no wall, no clear marker. 

- This area is not forbidden — but family members other than Shūmaru rarely come this far
- The mountain above is considered to belong to no one, and to everyone
- There may be a stream somewhere above — its sound audible before it is visible

## The Household

- **Shūmaru's father** — high-ranking, likely periodically absent under the sankin-kōtai system (alternating between estate and Edo). A formal, correct man.
- **Shūmaru's mother** — present, managing the household. Her relationship with Shūmaru is warm but conducted within the forms of the household.
- **Servants** — a substantial household staff. Some families have been in service for generations.
- **A tutor** — a Confucian scholar, possibly living in the household. Shūmaru's primary educator.
- **A gardener** — old, mostly silent. Perhaps the person on the estate most comfortable with the shrine's proximity.

## The Shrine's History

*Deliberately vague — to be developed only as needed.*

The family has maintained the shrine for as long as records exist, and records do not go back to its founding. Local tradition holds that a fox spirit has protected this ground since before the current family arrived. The spirit is propitious — associated with the land's fertility and the family's continued good fortune.

The family makes offerings at the shrine on appropriate occasions. This is unremarkable — simply correct behavior toward a protective spirit.

Nobody speaks of the shrine in terms of mystery or danger. It is simply there, as the mountain is there.

## Seasonal Notes

The estate's character changes dramatically with the seasons — important for the story's texture.

| Season | Character |
|--------|-----------|
| **Spring** | Snow melts, the valley becomes visible again. Sudden green. Cherry or plum blossom brief and intense. |
| **Summer** | Deep green, humid, the forest close and dark. Insects loud at night. |
| **Autumn** | Shūmaru's season. The maples turn. Mist returns to the valley. The light becomes particular — clear, slightly melancholy. |
| **Winter** | Snow buries the garden. The shrine is half-hidden. The estate turns inward. The path to the shrine is Shūmaru's alone — no one else's footprints in the snow. |
