## Part One: The Kami

### What the Kami Are

The word **kami** (神) is usually translated as "god" or "spirit," but neither quite captures it. Kami are the sacred presences that inhabit the world — not remote divine beings but immanent ones, dwelling within natural phenomena, specific places, and certain animals. A mountain, a waterfall, an unusually old tree, a distinctive rock, a fox — all may be the residence of a kami, or may themselves be kami.

This is not metaphor in the Japanese worldview. It is simply how the world is structured. The Shinto understanding of reality is one in which the sacred and the ordinary exist in the same space, touching at countless points. This is why a boy like Shūmaru — who simply notices things, without distinguishing between the natural and the supernatural — is not unusual in his world's terms. He is simply attentive to what is actually there.

Kami can be benevolent or dangerous, protective or indifferent. They are not moral in the human sense. They are powerful, they have their own natures, and they respond to proper respect and offering.

### Offerings and Approach

The standard approach to a shrine involves:
- Purifying oneself — at minimum washing hands and mouth at the water basin (*temizuya*) before the shrine gate
- Making a small monetary offering
- Ringing the bell or clapping hands twice to alert the kami
- Bowing in prayer
- A final clap to end

Simple food offerings at private estate shrines: rice, sake, seasonal vegetables, fish, salt. Fried tofu (*aburaage*) is specifically associated with fox shrines — it is considered the fox's favorite food, which is why the rice dish *inari-zushi* (rice stuffed in fried tofu) takes the shrine's name.

### Purity and Pollution

Shinto places enormous emphasis on purity — **kegare** (pollution or defilement) must be avoided or cleansed. Things considered polluting include death, disease, blood, and certain other conditions. This is why Buddhist temples, not Shinto shrines, handle funerary rites. A priest must undergo purification (*misogi*) before rituals.

For the story: this has an implication for encounters with the kitsune. The kami world operates by different rules of purity than the human world. Shūmaru's willingness to simply be present at the stone circle — without performing, without asking, without seeking — may be understood as a form of natural purity.

## Part Two: Inari

### Who Inari Is

Inari Ōkami (稲荷大神) is the Japanese kami of foxes, fertility, rice, tea, sake, agriculture and industry, and general prosperity and worldly success — one of the principal kami of Shinto. Inari can be represented as male, female, or androgynous, and is sometimes seen as a collective of three or five individual kami.

The name derives from *ine* (rice seedling) — Inari is literally the "rice carrier" or "rice bearer." In earlier Japan, Inari was above all the protector of rice cultivation — which meant the protector of survival itself, since rice was the foundation of the entire economy and food supply.

By the 16th century, Inari had become the patron of blacksmiths and the protector of warriors, and worship of Inari spread across Japan in the Edo period. In the Tokugawa period, when money replaced rice as the measure of wealth in Japan, Inari's role as a kami of worldly prosperity was expanded to include all aspects of finance, business, and industry.

This adaptability is one of Inari's defining characteristics — a deity who moves with human needs and changing circumstances while remaining recognizably itself.

### Inari's Many Faces

Inari's flexibility reflects their many roles across Japanese society, from guardian of the rice fields to divine patron of merchants, swordsmiths, and blacksmiths. Inari can be male, female, both, or neither — this fluid identity is one of the unique aspects of their mythology.

Inari appears in artwork as: an old man carrying sheaves of rice; a young woman with flowing hair; a Buddhist bodhisattva; a fox. All are valid representations.

### Inari's Relationship to Foxes

Crucially, in orthodox Shinto tradition, **foxes are not Inari** — they are Inari's messengers (*kenzoku*). The confusion is ancient, however, and even within Japan has been present for centuries. Fox statues adorn all Inari shrines, and in popular culture the two figures have become intertwined. But in Shinto tradition, the fox serves Inari — it is not Inari.

The distinction matters for our story. The kitsune of the Kiriyama shrine is not Inari — it is older than the Inari tradition that now frames it, and it predates the name. It may once have been understood as a messenger of something even older. Gen'nosuke's scholarship approaches this question.

### Inari Shrines

There are an estimated 80,000 shrines in Japan, and over one-third — some 30,000 — are associated with Inari, making Inari shrines the most numerous type in the country.

An Inari shrine is identified by:
- **Vermillion torii gates** — sometimes multiple, in rows
- **Fox statues** (*kitsune*) flanking the entrance, usually in pairs — one male, one female. They typically hold symbolic objects: a jewel (*hoshi no tama*), a key to the rice granary, a scroll, a sheaf of rice, or a fox cub.
- **Offerings** of rice, sake, and fried tofu (*aburaage*)
- A **sacred rope** (*shimenawa*) marking the threshold between ordinary and sacred space

The fox statues at the Kiriyama shrine hold their features softened by weather and moss — the symbolic objects they once held are no longer clearly identifiable. One sits slightly higher than the other.

### Mythology of Inari's Origin

According to legend, Inari was first worshipped on Mount Inari from the 8th century CE following a discovery by a man called Hata no Irogu, who followed a bird's flight to a mountain peak and found rice growing there. The monk Kūkai (Kōbō Daishi, 774–835 CE) claimed to have met an old man carrying rice on Inari mountain and considered it a meeting with the god himself.

A mythological account of how foxes became Inari's servants: A fox family journeyed to Inari Mountain, where they offered their service. Their loyalty was accepted and they became the shrine's guardians and messengers.

## Part Three: The Kitsune

### What Kitsune Are

Every red fox trotting through the forests of Honshu is a kitsune. But in mythology and folklore, the term carries weight that no English translation captures. A kitsune is a fox that has lived long enough, and grown powerful enough, to cross the boundary between animal and spirit. The earliest written references appear in the Nihon Shoki (Chronicles of Japan), compiled in 720 AD.

Kitsune possess **bakeru** — the ability to transform shape and appearance — and **bakasu** — the ability to beguile or bewitch. These are not tricks exactly; they are expressions of the fox's nature, which is inherently liminal, existing between states.

### The Tails

In Japanese folklore, kitsune have as many as nine tails. Generally, a greater number of tails indicates an older and more powerful kitsune; some folktales say that a fox will only grow additional tails after it has lived 100 years. One, five, seven, and nine tails are the most common numbers in folktales.

A nine-tailed fox — **kyūbi no kitsune** (九尾の狐) — has lived at least 900 years and possesses near-godlike abilities. These represent the pinnacle of fox power, possessing near-godlike abilities including omniscience, perfect shapeshifting, and control over natural elements.

At this level of age and power, a fox living a thousand years gains nine tails, white or golden fur, and the ability to see and hear anything happening anywhere in the world.

For our story: the Kiriyama kitsune is of very great age — certainly many centuries, possibly far older if connected to the Jōmon site. Its number of tails, if it has them in its fox form, is not specified. This is deliberate. The nature of something this old resists easy categorization.

### Types of Kitsune

**Zenko** (善狐) — good foxes, divine foxes, servants of Inari. Benevolent, protective, acting as messengers between the celestial and human worlds. The white fox (*byakko*) is the classic form of the zenko, guardian of Inari shrines.

**Yako / Nogitsune** (野狐) — field foxes, wild foxes, operating independently of divine service. Not inherently evil, but outside the moral framework of divine authority. Mischievous, unpredictable, following their own purposes.

**Tenko** (天狐) — celestial foxes, the highest category of kitsune, hundreds of years old. They live in the sky and appear as humanlike female figures. All-powerful within their domain.

The Kiriyama kitsune defies easy classification. It is associated with the shrine and its protective function — which suggests zenko. But it predates the Inari framework that defines zenko — which suggests something older than the classification system itself.

### Shapeshifting

According to many folktales, a kitsune learns how to shapeshift into human form at the age of 50 or 100 years old. However, this task often requires preparation. The Chinese version introduced the idea of kitsune transforming into beautiful women to seduce young men. However, in Japanese stories, these foxes were often known as devoted wives who only left after being discovered or remembering their lives as foxes.

**Tells** — signs that may reveal a kitsune in human form:
- Shadows or reflections may reveal fox ears, tail, or entire fox form
- Fear or drunkenness may cause the disguise to slip momentarily
- They have trouble hiding their tails, so a careless fox or one drunk on sake may accidentally show it and expose their identity
- Very old kitsune speak in an antiquated way — they have not interacted with humans often enough to keep up with language change
- Some sources suggest that a very religious or spiritually perceptive person may see the kitsune's true nature simply by looking

For our story: Shūmaru has glimpsed something twice — when Yukino is very still, listening to something he cannot hear, her eyes for a moment hold a quality that is not human. He looked away both times before he could be certain. The reflection tell is potentially significant — the pond in the garden, the pool at the stone circle.

### The Hoshi no Tama — Star Ball

It is said that any kitsune has a round-shaped gem called *hoshi no tama* ("star ball"). The gem is said to hold the owner's magic power, and the kitsune will die if separated from it for too long.

The hoshi no tama contains a portion of the kitsune's soul — its very essence and magical power made manifest. When in fox form, a kitsune keeps the ball in its mouth or carries it on its tail. When in human form, it may take the form of a jewel or piece of jewelry.

Those who obtain the ball may extract a promise from the kitsune to help them in exchange for its return. A 12th-century tale describes a man who obtained a fox's star ball; the fox eventually pleaded: *"If you don't give it back, I'll be your enemy forever. If you do give it back, I'll stick to you like a protector god."* The man returned it, and the fox later saved his life.

For our story: the stone fox statues at the shrine — like all Inari fox representations — hold objects in their mouths or beneath their paws. The objects at the Kiriyama shrine are no longer clearly identifiable beneath moss and weather. One of them may once have been a jewel. This is the kind of detail Gen'nosuke's dead teacher noticed and wrote about.

### Kitsune-bi — Fox Fire

Kitsune are associated with mysterious lights — **kitsune-bi** (狐火), fox fire — which appear in the forest at night, will-o'-the-wisp-like. These are sometimes said to be kitsune holding their star balls, or performing rituals, or simply moving through the dark forest. They appear in folktales as disorienting, leading travelers astray.

For our story: on certain autumn evenings, there is sometimes a faint light among the trees above the shrine. The household has noticed this over the years. The gardener has an explanation for visitors who ask. He says it is the mountain.

### Famous Kitsune Stories

**Kuzunoha** — the most celebrated fox-wife tale in Japanese tradition. Abe no Yasuna rescues a white fox being hunted in the Shinoda woods. A woman named Kuzunoha appears and nurses him to health. They marry and have a son. One day her true form is revealed — she was the fox he saved. She cannot stay. She writes her farewell poem on a paper door with a brush held in her mouth: "If you love me, come and visit, in the forest of Shinoda in Izumi, the resentful kudzu leaf." Their son became Abe no Seimei, the most famous onmyōji (court astrologer and sorcerer) in Japanese history.

The Kuzunoha story was adapted into a puppet play (*bunraku*) in 1734 — well within the Genroku period that frames our story. Gen'nosuke would know it. In the context of our story, its themes are directly relevant: the fox who takes human form, the relationship with a human, the eventual departure, the half-human child who inherits something of both worlds.

**Tamamo-no-Mae** — a darker tale. A nine-tailed kitsune infiltrated the imperial court disguised as an impossibly beautiful and learned woman, nearly killing the emperor through proximity before being exposed. Her body after death became the *Sessho-seki* ("Killing Stone"), which emitted poisonous gases. The Edo period expanded this tale across civilizations.

**The Fox Wife of Ono** — a gentler tale. A man saved a wounded fox. Years later a beautiful woman appeared and became his wife. When his old fox companion appeared and the wife's shadow revealed fox ears, he realized the truth. Unlike other tales, Ono accepted his wife's nature, and they lived together until her natural death. Love transcending the divide between human and fox.

This last story is the one most relevant to our purposes.

### Kitsune and Kagura Dance

Sacred dance — **kagura** (神楽) — is performed at shrines as offerings to the kami. The miko's dance (*miko-mai*) is a formalized version of what was once a more directly shamanic practice — the body becoming a vessel for divine presence, the dancer mediating between human and spirit worlds.

In the older, pre-Edo tradition, before the Tokugawa suppressed shamanic practices, a miko might enter trance, speak for the kami directly, receive messages. By our period, this has been officially formalized into ceremony rather than shamanism — but the older practice persists in rural areas, quietly, in the women who still have the ability.

Yukino dancing kagura alone at the stone circle at dawn — performing an offering to whatever inhabits the stones, which is herself — is not paradoxical in the spirit world's terms. She contains the stone circle's presence the way a river contains the water flowing through it.

## Part Four: Implications for the Story

### The Kitsune's Classification Problem

The Kiriyama kitsune resists classification. It is:
- Associated with a protective shrine function — which suggests *zenko*, a divine fox serving Inari
- Older than the Inari tradition — which suggests something prior to classification
- Connected to the Jōmon stone circle — which places it in a stratum of Japanese spiritual life that precedes the formal Shinto tradition itself

Gen'nosuke's dead teacher suspected this. His notes contain references to spirits that predate their own names — kami whose association with Inari, or with any named deity, was a later overlay on something much older. This is heterodox scholarship in the Edo period context. It questions the received framework.

### The Fox Wife Theme and Our Story

The *kitsune-tsuma* (fox wife) tradition is the closest narrative model for what passes between Shūmaru and Yukino — but with significant differences:

- Traditional fox wife stories involve adult relationships; ours involves children
- Traditional fox wife stories end with exposure and departure; ours may too, but the mechanism is different
- Traditional fox wife stories often center on deception; ours centers on recognition
- The Kuzunoha model — departure as inevitability, not betrayal — is the closest parallel

The question our story holds: *what does recognition look like, and what does it cost?*

### The Hoshi no Tama and Shūmaru

The star ball — the kitsune's soul made physical — is never mentioned in our story directly. But its symbolic dimension is present. What Shūmaru holds, in his private knowledge of Yukino, is something like a star ball: a portion of the being's essence, trusted to him. The knowledge does not give him power over her. It gives him responsibility.

Whether he is equal to that responsibility is the story's deepest question about him.

### What the Tradition Tells Us About Endings

Fox stories end in a limited number of ways:
1. **Exposure and flight** — the fox is discovered and cannot remain in human form; she leaves, often writing a farewell poem
2. **Willing departure** — the fox chooses to leave, for reasons the human cannot fully understand
3. **The fox returns** — after leaving, the fox visits again in its true form, or is seen by the human in the forest
4. **Death** — the fox dies, sometimes leaving behind a human child, sometimes not
5. **Persistence** — like the Fox Wife of Ono, love holds the relationship in being across the boundary

Our story's ending is not yet determined. But the tradition suggests that departure, in some form, is almost inevitable — not as tragedy necessarily, but as the natural shape of a relationship between a human and something that does not age the way humans age.

What Shūmaru does with that departure — how he carries it — may be the story's final question.
