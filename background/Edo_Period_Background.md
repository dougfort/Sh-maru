# Background Reference: Middle Edo Period Japan
## For the Shūmaru Project — circa 1688–1720

---

## The Bigger Picture

The Edo period (1603–1868) takes its name from the city of Edo — modern-day Tokyo — which became the seat of the Tokugawa shogunate. Japan was governed not by the Emperor, who remained a figurehead in Kyoto, but by the **shogun**, a supreme military ruler. Beneath the shogun were the **daimyo** — roughly 250 regional lords — each governing their own domain (called a *han*). Beneath the daimyo were layers of samurai retainers.

The period Shūmaru inhabits — approximately 1688–1720 — overlaps almost exactly with the celebrated **Genroku era** (1688–1704), often described as Japan's cultural peak, a kind of High Renaissance. After nearly a century of Tokugawa peace, prosperity had accumulated, and it now expressed itself in art, literature, theatre, and refined living.

By 1700, Edo was almost certainly the largest city in the world, with around 1.2 million inhabitants — compared to perhaps 800,000 in London at the time. Japan as a whole had a population of roughly 30 million.

---

## The Social Order

Edo society was organized into a strict hereditary hierarchy. Movement between classes was essentially impossible. The four official classes, in descending order, were:

**Samurai** — warriors and administrators, the ruling class. About 6% of the population. They alone had the right to carry swords.

**Farmers** (*hyakushō*) — about 80% of the population. Respected in theory (they produced the rice that was the basis of all wealth), but heavily taxed and bound to the land.

**Artisans** (*shokunin*) — craftspeople, makers of goods.

**Merchants** (*chōnin*) — the lowest official class, regarded as parasites who produced nothing. Ironically, merchants often became the wealthiest people in Japan during this period, sometimes lending money to impoverished samurai.

Outside this system: Buddhist monks, Shinto priests, the imperial court nobility in Kyoto, and outcaste groups.

Shūmaru's family sits near the top of the samurai hierarchy — perhaps **hatamoto** (direct retainers of the shogun) or high-ranking retainers of a major daimyo. This places them among the most privileged few percent of the population.

---

## The Samurai in Peacetime

Here is a central irony of the period: the samurai were a warrior class living through an extended era of profound peace. By 1688, there had been no significant warfare in Japan for nearly 70 years.

The result was a class simultaneously at the height of its prestige and somewhat adrift from its original purpose. Samurai increasingly became civil servants and administrators. They practiced martial arts, but largely as discipline and ritual rather than preparation for actual combat.

Upper-ranking samurai devoted considerable energy to what might be called the arts of civilization: the **tea ceremony** (*chado*), **flower arrangement** (*ikebana*), **calligraphy**, **Noh drama**, **poetry**, and the cultivation of refined gardens. These were not mere hobbies — they were expressions of status and moral cultivation, deeply bound up with Confucian ideals of the gentleman-scholar.

The great samurai estates maintained elaborate household rituals, formal reception rooms, and strict protocols governing everything from how one entered a room to how one held a cup.

---

## Confucianism and Buddhism

Two intellectual systems shaped the inner life of the period, and both would have been part of Shūmaru's upbringing.

**Neo-Confucianism** was the official philosophical framework of the Tokugawa state. It emphasized: loyalty to one's lord, filial piety (respect for parents and ancestors), social hierarchy as natural order, self-discipline, and the cultivation of virtue through study. A boy of Shūmaru's class would be immersed in Confucian texts.

**Buddhism** remained important, particularly in the upper classes. Buddhist temples served practical functions too — record-keeping, funeral rites, and as repositories of learning.

**Shinto** — the native Japanese spiritual tradition — coexisted comfortably with both. It was less a formal doctrine than a pervasive sense of the sacred in nature: in rivers, mountains, trees, foxes, and the spirits (*kami*) inhabiting the world. A rural estate would almost certainly maintain a Shinto shrine.

These three traditions did not feel contradictory to most people. They addressed different aspects of life: Confucianism governed social and moral conduct, Buddhism addressed death and the afterlife, Shinto maintained the relationship between the living and the spirit world.

## The Genroku Cultural Moment

The era name *Genroku* literally means "original happiness" — an apt description of its mood. The arts flourished as they rarely had before or after.

**Literature:** The poet **Matsuo Bashō** (1644–1694) brought haiku to its classical peak — spare, nature-rooted, luminous. His *Oku no Hosomichi* ("Narrow Road to the Deep North," 1689) is one of the great works of Japanese literature. The novelist **Ihara Saikaku** (1642–1693) depicted urban life with wit and realism. Both were active in the decades just before Shūmaru's story.

**Theatre:** **Kabuki** and **bunraku** (puppet theatre) developed into sophisticated art forms. The playwright **Chikamatsu Monzaemon** (1653–1724) — sometimes called Japan's Shakespeare — was writing his greatest works during this exact period.

**Visual art:** Woodblock printing (*ukiyo-e*) produced popular images of actors, courtesans, and landscapes. The decorative **Rinpa** school produced exquisite paintings of nature — gold leaf, bold designs, stylized flowers and birds. These would have decorated screens and scrolls in an estate like Shūmaru's.

**Music:** The **koto** (a plucked string instrument) and **shamisen** (a three-stringed lute) were the refined instruments of the period. The shakuhachi (bamboo flute) was associated with Zen Buddhism.

For Shūmaru's rural upper-class household, the most relevant arts would be: Noh drama (more aristocratic and austere than Kabuki), the tea ceremony, calligraphy, and poetry — particularly haiku and the older *waka* tradition.

## Education for a Boy of Shūmaru's Class

A high-ranking samurai boy's education was intensive and began young — typically around age seven.

The curriculum included:

**Chinese and Japanese classics** — read aloud, memorized, copied repeatedly. The Confucian *Analects* and similar texts were foundational.

**Calligraphy** — considered both an art and a moral discipline. The quality of one's writing reflected the quality of one's character.

**Poetry composition** — *waka* (classical 31-syllable verse) was an essential accomplishment. A samurai who could not compose a decent poem was considered uncultivated.

**Martial arts** — even in peacetime, sword practice (*kenjutsu*), archery (*kyūdō*), and horsemanship were maintained as disciplines. A boy would begin these around age seven or eight.

**Etiquette and deportment** — how to enter rooms, bow, address superiors, conduct oneself at formal occasions. This was as rigorously taught as anything else.

The formal coming-of-age ceremony (**genpuku**, 元服) typically occurred around age sixteen or seventeen, at which point a boy received his adult topknot hairstyle and his adult name. Until then, Shūmaru would be known by his childhood name.

Education at the highest levels was often conducted at home with private tutors (*jusha*), rather than at domain schools. A hatamoto's son might have a Confucian scholar living in the household.

## The Rural Estate

While the great samurai households of the period were concentrated in castle towns and Edo, some high-ranking families maintained rural estates. These might serve as:

- The family's ancestral home, maintained while the head of household alternated between it and Edo under the **sankin-kōtai** system of alternate attendance
- A retreat from urban life
- The seat of local administrative authority over surrounding farming villages

A substantial rural estate of this period would typically include:

**The main house** (*omoya*) — built of wood, with sliding paper screens (*shōji*), tatami mat floors, formal reception rooms, and interior gardens visible from the rooms. Low, horizontal, close to the ground.

**Gardens** — carefully designed, with raked gravel, stones, ponds, and shaped trees. Not wild, but nature organized into a contemplative form.

**Subsidiary buildings** — for retainers, servants, kitchens, storage.

**The shrine** — families of this status often maintained a private Shinto shrine on their grounds, sometimes dedicated to an Inari deity (a fox spirit, associated with rice and prosperity). These shrines could be very old, predating the current family's occupation.

**The boundary** — where the cultivated estate gives way to wilder ground: bamboo groves, woodland, the uncultivated. This liminal edge was understood as a place where the boundary between the human and spirit worlds was thinner.

## The Spirit World in Daily Life

For people of this period, the spirit world was not separate from ordinary life — it was woven into it. The *kami* (divine spirits) inhabited natural features: mountains, rivers, old trees, unusual rocks, certain animals. Foxes in particular were sacred to Inari, one of the most widely venerated Shinto deities.

**Inari shrines** were (and remain) the most numerous type of Shinto shrine in Japan. They were associated with rice, agriculture, fertility, and prosperity — appropriate for any estate whose wealth derived from land. The characteristic features of an Inari shrine include **torii gates** (often multiple, in a row) painted vermillion red, and **fox statues** (*kitsune*) flanking the entrance.

**Kitsune** (foxes) in Japanese folklore are complex figures:
- Long-lived, intelligent, and capable of shapeshifting into human form
- Messengers of Inari, and therefore sacred and protective
- Also associated with mischief, illusion, and the uncanny
- Believed to gain power and additional tails with age — a nine-tailed fox is ancient and immensely powerful
- Sometimes depicted as forming deep bonds with particular humans or households across generations

A family with a long-established Inari shrine might genuinely believe their estate to be under the protection of a fox spirit — not as superstition, but as straightforward fact.

---

## Isolation from the Outside World

One feature of the period worth noting: Japan was almost entirely closed to the outside world. The policy of **sakoku** ("locked country"), established in 1635, meant that Japanese were forbidden to travel abroad, and foreigners were forbidden to enter Japan. The sole exception was a small Dutch trading post on the artificial island of Dejima in Nagasaki.

For Shūmaru on his rural estate, the world beyond Japan was essentially non-existent. His entire universe was bounded by his estate, the nearest castle town, and the accumulated wisdom of Chinese and Japanese tradition. This creates a profound inwardness — the world of the story is sealed, complete, sufficient to itself.

## Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Shogun** | Supreme military ruler of Japan |
| **Daimyo** | Regional lord, governing a *han* (domain) |
| **Hatamoto** | Direct retainer of the shogun — high status |
| **Samurai** | Warrior-administrator class |
| **Ronin** | A masterless samurai |
| **Genpuku** (元服) | Coming-of-age ceremony for samurai boys |
| **Yōmyō** (幼名) | Childhood name, used until genpuku |
| **Tatami** | Woven straw floor mats — standard flooring |
| **Shōji** | Sliding paper screens dividing rooms |
| **Torii** | Vermillion gate marking the entrance to a shrine |
| **Kami** | Divine spirits inhabiting the natural world |
| **Kitsune** | Fox; sacred to Inari, capable of shapeshifting |
| **Inari** | Shinto deity of rice, fertility, and prosperity |
| **Sankin-kōtai** | System requiring daimyo to alternate between Edo and their domain |
| **Haiku** | 17-syllable nature poem, perfected by Bashō |
| **Waka** | Classical 31-syllable Japanese verse |
| **Noh** | Austere, masked theatrical form patronized by the upper classes |
| **Kabuki** | More popular dramatic form, associated with urban culture |
| **Ukiyo-e** | Woodblock print art depicting the "floating world" |
| **Chado** | The way of tea; the tea ceremony |
| **Ikebana** | The art of flower arrangement |
| **Ma** (間) | The aesthetic concept of meaningful space and silence |
| **Bushido** | The way of the warrior — the samurai ethical code |
