# Daily Life at 霧の上 Kiri no Ue
## Routine and Rhythm — Shūmaru Project

---

## Overview

The day at Kiri no Ue is structured, unhurried, and governed by the rhythms of season and household rank. There are no clocks in the modern sense — time is marked by the position of the sun, the ringing of temple bells from the valley below, the movements of servants, and the body's own knowledge of the hour.

The household rises early and retires not long after dark. Lamplight — oil lamps, candles — is expensive and used carefully. Winter shortens the day considerably. The mountain location means weather shapes routine in ways it would not in the city.

---

## The Shape of Shūmaru's Day

### Before Dawn — Shūmaru's Alone
Shūmaru wakes before anyone else. This is simply his nature — he has always done this.

He lies on his futon in the dark, listening. The cryptomeria when there is wind. The particular silence of snow. Occasionally, when the wind is exactly right, the distant sound of the stream above the shrine. Gen'nosuke's incense, if it has begun — the smell reaching the corridor, telling him the tutor has already risen.

When the sky begins to lighten — gray at first, then the faintest pale gold through the shōji — he rises. He rolls his futon, puts on his robe. In winter he wraps himself carefully against the cold of the corridors.

**This is when he goes to the shrine.** Not every morning — but often. The path through the cryptomeria in the half-dark, the mist heaviest in the valley below, the shrine emerging as he rounds the path's single bend. He makes a small offering — a cup of water, a handful of rice — and stands for a while in the absolute stillness of that place.

He is back before the household stirs.

### Dawn — The Household Wakes
Servants rise first. The kitchen comes alive — the sound of the wood-burning *kamado* stove being lit, water being fetched, rice being set to cook. The smell of woodsmoke and then, gradually, the particular smell of rice steaming.

Ume rises and oversees the morning. Her movements through the house are quiet and purposeful.

Gen'nosuke's formal sitting practice — *zazen* before his altar — takes place at this hour. Twenty minutes, perhaps. The incense burns.

### Morning — Breakfast
Breakfast in the Edo period was usually around seven, eaten on tatami floors. At Kiri no Ue, for a household of this rank, it would be served on individual lacquered trays — *ozen* — each person receiving their own portion.

A typical morning meal:
- Steamed white rice — the foundation of every meal, served in generous quantities
- Miso soup — *misoshiru* — with tofu, seaweed, or seasonal vegetables
- Pickled vegetables — *tsukemono* — essential at every meal
- Perhaps grilled fish, or a small dish of simmered vegetables
- Green tea

The meal is eaten in relative quiet. Correct posture is maintained. Shūmaru eats with his customary economy of movement, not hurrying, not lingering.

Mitsumaru, when present, eats with the easy appetite of a growing boy who inhabits his body comfortably. Their father, when present, eats in formal silence. Ume oversees without appearing to.

### Late Morning — Lessons with Gen'nosuke
The most substantial portion of Shūmaru's structured day. Lessons typically run for two to three hours.

**What is studied:**
- The Confucian classics — reading aloud, memorization, discussion
- Calligraphy — sustained practice, copying texts in both Chinese and Japanese scripts
- Classical Japanese literature — poetry anthologies, sections of *The Tale of Genji*
- Poetry composition — attempting *waka* and increasingly *haiku*
- History — Chinese dynastic history, Japanese chronicles

**How Gen'nosuke teaches:**
Quietly. He does not lecture at length. He assigns, observes, asks questions that have no simple answer. Silence is permitted — welcomed, even. Lessons sometimes proceed for long periods with both of them simply reading, the only sound the wind outside and the occasional scratch of brush on paper.

He offers kōans occasionally — not on a schedule, never announced as such. They arrive as questions dropped into silence. *If you meet the Buddha on the road, kill him. What was your face before your parents were born?* Shūmaru receives them without visible confusion. He goes quiet and inward, as if listening. Gen'nosuke watches this carefully.

The study room contains: low writing desks, ink stones, brush rests, stacks of texts. A small window looks toward the side garden. In good weather the shōji is open to the sound of the garden.

### Midday — Lunch and Free Time
During the Edo period, people generally ate two meals a day in the earlier period; midway through, three meals a day became the norm. By the Genroku period, a wealthy household like the Kiriyama's would likely eat three modest meals. Midday is the simplest — cold rice left from morning, miso soup, pickles, perhaps a small side dish.

After the midday meal, Shūmaru has a period of relative freedom. This is variable — sometimes it is claimed by additional lessons or practice, sometimes it is genuinely his own.

**Where he goes:**

*The garden* — he sits at the edge of the veranda, looking at the pond. Not doing nothing — his mind is active. He composes lines of poetry in his head. He watches the reflections in the water. He notices when the light changes.

*The stables* — particularly when weather makes the garden uninviting. The warmth, the smell, the unhurried company of large animals. He watches the grooms at their work. He visits his particular horse.

*His room* — reading, or simply being still. His few collected objects on the cabinet: the smooth stone, the cryptomeria cone, whatever else has gathered there.

*The edge, and beyond* — if the afternoon is his and the weather is clear, he sometimes goes back to the shrine, or simply into the trees. He is never gone long enough to cause concern. Nobody asks where he has been.

### Afternoon — Martial Training
Several afternoons a week, Shūmaru has martial training. At his age this means primarily:

**馬術 *Bajutsu*** — horsemanship, under the head groom's supervision. Learning to mount, to sit correctly, to guide the horse. He is a capable student — his natural stillness serves him here. Horses respond to quietness.

**剣術 *Kenjutsu*** — sword practice with a wooden *bokken*. Supervised by a martial arts instructor — either a retainer of the household or someone who comes from the nearest castle town. The forms (*kata*) are practiced repeatedly. Shūmaru executes them correctly. The instructor finds him technically precise but occasionally notices something slightly absent in the boy's attention — as if part of him is elsewhere.

**弓道 *Kyūdō*** — archery, begun more recently. The ritual of it — the precise placement of feet, the specific way the bow is drawn, the moment of release — suits Shūmaru more than sword practice. There is something in the held stillness before release that he understands without being taught.

### Late Afternoon — Household and Cultural Arts
The later hours of the afternoon shade into household life:

- Calligraphy practice, if not completed in the morning
- Occasionally, observation of or participation in the tea ceremony — Ume maintains this practice; on certain afternoons Shūmaru is invited to observe
- Music — a *koto* teacher visits periodically; Shūmaru learns the basics dutifully without particular feeling for it. He is more drawn to the sound of Gen'nosuke's shakuhachi, which he has never mentioned.
- Reading — his own, in the household library or his room

### Dusk — Evening Meal
The evening meal was a cornerstone of family life — a time for the entire household to gather, the atmosphere more relaxed than formal occasions while still maintaining decorum.

At Kiri no Ue, when the father is absent — which is often — the evening meal is Ume, Mitsumaru, and Shūmaru. Served on individual trays on the tatami. The meal is warmer and more substantial than the midday:

- Fresh steamed rice
- Miso soup
- Grilled fish — the mountain location means freshwater fish: trout, sweetfish (*ayu*) in season
- Simmered seasonal vegetables
- Pickles
- Green tea

Conversation at the evening meal is quiet but present. Ume draws out both boys in her careful way. Mitsumaru talks readily — about his lessons, his riding, whatever is occupying him. Shūmaru listens more than he speaks, but his attention is complete. Occasionally he says something that stops the conversation briefly — an observation so precise it requires a moment to absorb.

### After Dark — Evening
Lamps are lit sparingly. The improvement of lanterns allowed people to move about at night, but at Kiri no Ue evenings are quiet and inward.

Gen'nosuke reads by lamplight in his quarters, or plays the shakuhachi. The sound drifts through the house, barely audible — not music exactly, more like the sound the house makes when it breathes.

Ume attends to household accounts, correspondence, or her own reading.

Shūmaru returns to his room. He reads if there is light enough. He practices calligraphy by lamplight occasionally — the characters emerging slowly in the small pool of light. When he puts out the lamp, he lies on his futon and listens.

Sleep comes gradually. He is not a boy who falls instantly unconscious. He descends into sleep the way he does most things — attentively, noticing each stage.

## Seasonal Variations

The daily routine shifts significantly with the seasons:

**Spring** — days lengthen rapidly. The valley below becomes visible as mist thins. More time outdoors. The garden demands attention — the gardener is busy. Shūmaru notices the exact day each spring when he can first hear the stream clearly from his room.

**Summer** — long days, the forest close and dark, insects loud at dusk and through the night. Morning lessons in the cool before heat builds. Shūmaru sometimes sleeps with his shōji open a crack, listening to the night sounds.

**Autumn** — Shūmaru's season. The maples turn. The light becomes particular — clear, slightly melancholy, everything seen with unusual precision. The mist returns to the valley in the mornings. He goes to the shrine more often in autumn. Something about the season feels like permission.

**Winter** — the day contracts. Snow muffles the estate. Indoor life intensifies — more reading, more calligraphy, the warmth of the kitchen and the stables valued more keenly. The path to the shrine is Shūmaru's alone — no other footprints in the snow. On very cold mornings the cryptomeria hold snow in their branches and occasionally release it in sudden soft falls, as if the tree has decided something.

## What This Tells Us About the Story

Shūmaru's day has **structured time** — lessons, martial training, meals — and **unstructured time** — the garden, the stables, the free afternoon, the early morning before anyone rises.

The story lives almost entirely in the **unstructured time**. The shrine visits. The free afternoons. The early mornings in the mist.

The structured time presses around it — lessons with Gen'nosuke that are gradually becoming something more than lessons, martial training that requires him to perform in ways that cost him something, the evening meal that is warm but can't reach what lies deepest in him.

Yukino, when she arrives, will belong to the unstructured time. The edges of the day. The threshold hours.
