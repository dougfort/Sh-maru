# The Year at 霧の上 Kiri no Ue
## Ritual Calendar and Seasonal Events — Shūmaru Project

---

## Overview

The Edo period operated on the **lunar calendar** — meaning dates fall roughly four to six weeks later than their modern equivalents. A note on time: there were no weeks, no days off in the modern sense. Time was marked by the position of the sun, the ringing of temple bells, the agricultural cycle, and the sequence of ceremonies and festivals that structured the year.

For a high-ranking samurai household on a rural mountain estate, the year's rhythm combined:
- **Shinto observances** — shrine ceremonies, purification rituals, seasonal offerings
- **Buddhist observances** — memorial services, equinox visits to ancestors
- **Confucian household rituals** — formal ceremonies expressing filial piety and family order
- **Seasonal agricultural markers** — rice planting, harvest, the mountain's own rhythms
- **Court and samurai ceremonial obligations** — formal observances appropriate to rank

The story moves through approximately one full year. What follows is the calendar of that year, month by month, with particular attention to what Shūmaru would experience and how each event might register in his particular interiority.

## Winter — The Story's Starting Point
*(Late December through January in the lunar calendar — approximately February in modern terms)*

### 大晦日 Ōmisoka — New Year's Eve
The last night of the old year. The household is already deeply prepared — a thorough cleaning (*ōsōji*, the great cleaning) has been underway for days, purifying the house of the old year's dust and disorder. The smell of clean wood and cold air.

Buddhist temple bells ring 108 times at midnight — one ring for each of the 108 worldly desires in Buddhist thought, releasing them from the old year. The sound carries through the mountain air. Even at Kiri no Ue, high above the valley, the bells from the nearest temple can be heard faintly, through the cold and the dark.

Shūmaru lies awake and counts them.

### 正月 Shōgatsu — New Year
Japan's most important festival. For a samurai household of the Kiriyama's rank, a deeply ceremonial time.

**Preparations visible in the house:**
- **門松 *kadomatsu*** — arrangements of pine, bamboo, and plum at the gate. Pine for longevity, bamboo for resilience, plum for perseverance. These are elaborate for a household of this rank.
- **注連飾り *shimekazari*** — sacred rope decorations of twisted straw hung at the entrance, marking the house as purified and ready to receive the year-god
- **鏡餅 *kagami mochi*** — two round stacked rice cakes, shaped like the ancient sacred mirrors, displayed in the tokonoma (decorative alcove). Topped with a daidai orange — written with characters meaning "over generations."

**New Year's Day observances:**
- Rising before dawn to see **初日の出 *hatsuhinode*** — the first sunrise of the year. Shūmaru does this alone, from the garden's edge, the mist still filling the valley below. The light comes up over the mountain to the east, touching the cryptomeria.
- The formal New Year greeting between family members — elaborate, formal, conducted in specific hierarchical order. Even in a warm household, this is conducted correctly.
- **お節料理 *osechi ryōri*** — the New Year feast. Prepared days in advance, served in tiered lacquer boxes (*jūbako*). Each dish symbolic: black soybeans (*kuromame*) for diligent work, herring roe (*kazunoko*) for fertility and offspring, burdock root (*gobō*) for deep roots, sweet chestnuts (*kurikinton*) for wealth.
- **お雑煮 *ozōni*** — mochi soup, each region's recipe distinct. The Kiriyama version would reflect the mountain province's character.
- **初詣 *hatsumōde*** — the first shrine visit of the year. The Kiriyama family makes offerings at their estate shrine. This is Shūmaru's first formal interaction with the shrine in the new year. He notes, as always, the quality of stillness there.

**January 2:**
- **書き初め *kakizome*** — the first calligraphy of the year. Shūmaru sits at his desk in the cold room, the ink freshly ground, and writes his first characters. Gen'nosuke has given him a phrase to copy — something from the classics, something appropriate. But Shūmaru writes it the way he does everything: with full attention, making each stroke complete before the next.

**January 7:**
- **七草粥 *nanakusa-gayu*** — seven-herb rice porridge, eaten to purify the stomach after New Year's excess and pray for health. The seven spring herbs: shepherd's purse, cudweed, nipplewort, water dropwort, chickweed, turnip, daikon. Quiet, restorative.

**Little New Year (January 15 by old reckoning):**
The New Year decorations are ceremonially burned so that the year-god's spirit can be welcomed freshly the following year.

## Early Spring
*(Second month of the lunar calendar — approximately March)*

### 節分 Setsubun — the Division of the Seasons
The last day of winter by the old calendar. The day before the traditional first day of spring.

**Bean throwing (*mamemaki*):** Roasted soybeans are thrown to drive out evil spirits (*oni*) and welcome good fortune, to the cry of "Oni wa soto! Fuku wa uchi!" ("Out with demons! In with good fortune!"). Even in a formal samurai household, this has a slightly festive, raucous quality — children are permitted a license not usually available to them.

Shūmaru participates correctly. He throws the beans with the precise force appropriate. But he is thinking about the oni — the demons. He has never quite believed in them the way the ceremony implies he should. What he has felt in the forest is something else entirely. Less categorical.

### 雛祭り Hinamatsuri — the Doll Festival (March 3)
Girls' Day. Not directly Shūmaru's festival — but the household observes it. Elaborate tiered displays of imperial court dolls (*hina ningyō*) are arranged on red-draped platforms. The dolls represent the Emperor and Empress, court ladies, musicians, ministers.

This is a quiet, feminine ceremony. Ume oversees it. Shūmaru observes from a respectful distance — the formal arrangement, the particular colors, the hierarchy expressed in who sits where.

### 春分 Shunbun — the Spring Equinox
**彼岸 *Higan*** — the spring equinox period. Seven days, with the equinox at the center. A Buddhist observance: visiting family graves, making offerings to ancestors, eating *botamochi* (rice cakes covered in sweet bean paste).

For a samurai family, this is a significant obligation. The Kiriyama ancestral graves — wherever they are kept — receive formal visits and offerings. Shūmaru accompanies the household. He is attentive during these ceremonies in his particular way — not pious exactly, but genuinely present to the idea that the dead are somewhere, and that ceremony keeps the connection properly maintained.

## Spring
*(Third month of the lunar calendar — approximately April)*

### 花見 Hanami — Flower Viewing
The cherry blossom season. On a mountain estate in Shinano, cherry blossoms come slightly later than in the lowlands. But they come.

The estate likely has plum trees (*ume*) that bloom first — in late winter, their flowers white and fragrant against the snow. These are Ume's flowers. Then, later, cherry.

**Flower viewing** in the Edo period is elevated to an art. A samurai household of the Kiriyama's rank would observe it with considerable refinement — the appropriate food prepared, the appropriate poems composed or recited, the blossoms appreciated with proper aesthetic consciousness.

For Shūmaru, the blossoms matter differently. He notices the precise day when the first plum opens. He notes the color of the sky behind the cherry when it is exactly right. He composes something in his head — too short, not quite there yet.

### The Return of the Valley
The mist in the valley below begins to thin with the warming. Shūmaru can see further and further down — the rice paddies becoming visible, then the rooftops of the farming villages, then finally the river. The world is returning after winter's erasure.

He notices the specific morning when the valley is fully visible for the first time.

---

## Late Spring — The Fox, the Circle
*(Fourth month of the lunar calendar — approximately May)*

### 端午の節句 Tango no Sekku — Boys' Day (5th day of 5th month)
The most directly relevant seasonal festival for Shūmaru. An important ceremony among samurai households.

**Displays:**
- The Kiriyama family flags and *hatasashimono* (family banner) are displayed outside
- Iris (*shōbu*) decorates the eaves — considered protective and associated with warrior spirit; the word *shōbu* also means "victory in martial arts"
- Inside, displays of samurai helmets, armor, and warrior figures

**The iris bath (*shōbuyu*):** Bathing in water infused with iris leaves — considered purifying and health-giving.

This festival has a particular resonance for Shūmaru as a younger son. The displays are the family's martial identity made visible — which is primarily Mitsumaru's inheritance. Shūmaru participates correctly. He knows the ceremony is not fundamentally about him, but he doesn't experience this as wound. Just clarity.

### The Fox, Early Summer
Somewhere in this period — late spring or very early summer — the fox appears on the path beyond the shrine. Shūmaru follows it. He finds the stone circle.

This is not a festival. It has no name. It is simply the day his world changed its shape.

## Early Summer
*(Fifth month of the lunar calendar — approximately June)*

### Deepening at the Circle
Shūmaru returns to the stone circle repeatedly through early summer. The forest is at its densest green. The stream runs clear and cold.

He shares the discovery with Gen'nosuke — carefully, obliquely at first. Gen'nosuke receives it with his characteristic stillness. They begin to speak about the kami, the old places, the nature of sacred ground. This is the deepening of their relationship.

### 夏越の祓 Nagoshi no Harae — the Great Summer Purification (June 30)
One of the two great semi-annual purification ceremonies in the Shinto calendar, held at the end of the sixth lunar month.

**The *chinowa*:** A large ring of woven cogon grass (*chigaya*) — taller than a person — is erected at the shrine entrance. Worshippers pass through it in a specific pattern: left, right, left — three passes, moving through the ring as if tracing a figure-eight. This passage purifies the impurities accumulated in the first half of the year.

For the Kiriyama estate shrine, this ceremony would be conducted by the visiting kannushi (priest) from the nearest castle town. The family attends. The *chinowa* is erected at the torii gate.

Shūmaru passes through it. The ring is woven grass but it feels like a threshold — which it is. He notices that passing through a ring of grass and emerging on the other side is not unlike what happens when he enters the forest. A change of kind, not just location.

**Hitogata:** Small paper figures cut in human shape — the worshipper writes their name on it, rubs it on their body to transfer impurities, and it is either burned or floated on water.

**Minazuki:** The traditional sweet of this ceremony — a triangle of steamed rice cake topped with sweet red beans. Served in the household after the shrine ceremony.

*Note: The Nagoshi no Harae falls very close to the summer solstice. In the story, these two thresholds — the purification ceremony and the solstice — are proximate. The purification ceremony at the shrine happens a day or two before the solstice. The next morning at the solstice, Shūmaru goes to the stone circle and sees the figure dancing.*

## Midsummer — The Glimpse
*(Sixth month of the lunar calendar — approximately July)*

### 夏至 Geshi — the Summer Solstice
The year at its fullest. The forest at its most dense. The pre-dawn hour of the solstice — the mist particularly luminous, the air thick and warm.

**This is when Shūmaru sees the figure dancing in the circle.**

See the story bible for the full account. She dances kagura through the mist. He cannot be certain of what he sees. He is permanently, deeply affected.

### 七夕 Tanabata — the Star Festival (7th day of 7th month)
The festival of the weaver star (Vega) and the cowherd star (Altair), separated by the Milky Way and permitted to meet only once a year, on this night, when magpies form a bridge between them.

**Customs:** Writing wishes on strips of colored paper (*tanzaku*) and hanging them on bamboo branches. Poetry composition. Gazing at the stars if the weather permits.

For Shūmaru, now carrying what he saw at the solstice, this festival — about two stars permitted one meeting per year — has a different weight than it did before. He writes his wish on the paper strip. We don't know what he writes. He hangs it on the bamboo with the others.

---

## Late Summer
*(Seventh month of the lunar calendar — approximately August)*

### お盆 Obon — the Festival of the Dead
The most important Buddhist observance after New Year. The spirits of the ancestors return for three days to visit the living family. Lanterns are lit to guide them home. Memorial services at Buddhist temples. Offerings at the family altar.

For a samurai household, this is a significant ceremonial period. The household Buddhist altar is prepared, offerings made, candles and incense burned.

**Bon odori** — festival dancing performed in many communities, though a formal samurai household would observe this more privately than commoners.

For Shūmaru, the idea of the dead returning — the boundary between worlds becoming permeable for three days — registers differently now. He has seen what he has seen at the solstice. The idea that the world has more layers than the visible is no longer purely theoretical.

---

## Autumn — The Decline
*(Eighth through tenth months of the lunar calendar — approximately September through November)*

### 秋分 Shūbun — the Autumn Equinox
**彼岸 *Higan*** again — this time in autumn. The same seven-day Buddhist observance as the spring equinox: visiting graves, making offerings, eating *ohagi* (autumn's version of the rice cakes, with slightly different preparation).

The maples are beginning to turn on the mountain slopes. The light is changing — lower, clearer, that particular autumn quality that Shūmaru knows as his own season.

### 十五夜 Jūgoya — Moon Viewing (15th day of 8th month)
**中秋の名月 *Chūshū no meigetsu*** — the harvest moon. The full moon of the eighth lunar month, considered the most beautiful of the year.

**Tsukimi** — moon viewing — is one of the most refined aesthetic practices of the period. The household arranges a viewing: *susuki* (Japanese pampas grass) in a vase, offerings of *tsukimi dango* (round rice dumplings arranged in a pyramid), seasonal vegetables and fruits. Poetry is composed or recited.

The moon reflected in the garden pond. Shūmaru sits at the veranda's edge. The valley below is full of moonlight, the mist briefly absent on this clear autumn night. He composes something in his head. Something closer now to the poem he is working toward.

### Autumn at the Stone Circle
He goes to the circle several times in autumn. The *shinki* is present as always. The figure is not. The stream is colder. The dacite columns are their most austere — the moss slightly drier, the dark grey showing through.

He sits on his stone for long periods. He is not waiting. He is doing something that has no exact name.

### 紅葉 Kōyō — Autumn Leaves
The maples turn on the mountain slopes — the estate's edge, the forest path, the clearing itself. The Japanese aesthetic of *kōyō* (autumn leaf-viewing) parallels *hanami* — the appreciation of beautiful, transient natural phenomena as a contemplative and poetic practice.

The cryptomeria don't turn color — they are evergreen. But at the clearing, where deciduous trees have taken hold in the light gap, the leaves turn. Shūmaru notices this particularly: the leaves turning gold and red at the stone circle, falling into the stream, moving past his feet.

*Mono no aware* — the pathos of things — is most intense for him in this period. Everything is beautiful and ending.

## Early Winter
*(Tenth through eleventh months of the lunar calendar — approximately November through December)*

### 七五三 Shichi-go-san — Seven-Five-Three (November 15)
Children aged seven, five, and three are taken to shrines to thank the kami for their health and pray for continued protection. Boys at ages three and five; girls at three and seven.

At the story's opening, Shūmaru is eight or nine — just past the age for this ceremony. But he would remember his own shichi-go-san, and he might observe the ceremony being performed for a younger child in the household or village — the formal dress, the shrine visit, the chitose-ame (thousand-year candy) in its long decorated bag.

### Winter Deepening
The estate turns inward. Snow arrives — the mountain location means considerable accumulation. The path to the shrine is Shūmaru's alone after the first heavy snow; no other footprints appear there.

He goes to the shrine still, less often than in autumn, but he goes. The garden is buried. The stone foxes wear caps of snow. The *shimenawa* rope is ice-rimmed.

He does not go to the stone circle often now. The path is harder, the snow deep. But he thinks about it constantly. The poem is almost there.

---

## Winter Solstice — The Ending
*(Eleventh month of the lunar calendar — approximately December)*

### 冬至 Tōji — the Winter Solstice
The darkest day. The year's turning point — maximum darkness, after which the light begins its slow return.

**Traditional observances:**
- **柚子湯 *yuzuyu*** — bathing in water containing yuzu citrus fruit. The yuzu floating in the hot bath, its fragrance filling the bathing room. Said to ward off illness and strengthen the body for winter. There is something purifying about it — the same impulse as the summer's *chinowa* ring, now expressed in warmth and fragrance.
- **かぼちゃ *kabocha*** — pumpkin or winter squash eaten on this day, said to bring good health through the winter
- **小豆粥 *azuki kayu*** — red bean porridge, eaten in some traditions to ward off evil

For the Kiriyama household: the yuzu bath, the appropriate foods, the household observances. A day that acknowledges darkness while pointing toward the light.

**Shūmaru at the stone circle:**

In the early morning, before the household observances, he goes to the circle one last time. The snow is deep. The dacite columns are black against white. The stream runs under ice at its edges — only the center moving, dark and clear.

He sits on his stone. He stays for a long time. The cold is specific and complete.

He has the poem. It is ready.

He takes it from inside his robe — written on a small piece of paper, the ink careful. He tucks it into a crevice of the columnar dacite where it will be sheltered from the wind and snow. He weights it with a smooth stone.

He stands. He does not look back.

He walks through the cryptomeria. The snow falls from a branch above him — a sudden soft release. He keeps walking.

The light is already, incrementally, returning.

---

## The Poem

Left at the stone circle on the winter solstice. Not yet written. It will arrive at the story's end, when we know exactly what the story has been.

What it must hold: the circle. Complete and not complete. Open enough for something to have passed through.

The character 円 (*en*) — circle, complete, round — may or may not appear directly. But its quality must be present.
