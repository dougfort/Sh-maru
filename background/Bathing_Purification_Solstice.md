## Part One: Bathing at Kiri no Ue

### The Origins of Japanese Bathing
The origins of Japanese bathing culture can be traced to the Shinto ritual purification of *kegare* — spiritual pollution or defilement. This condition can be remedied through purification rites called *misogi* and *harae*. Bathing in Japan is therefore not merely hygienic — it is fundamentally spiritual, an act of cleansing that addresses the body and something beyond the body simultaneously.

Japanese elite began to construct private baths in their residences during the Kamakura period (1185–1333). By the Edo period, a high-ranking samurai household like the Kiriyama's would certainly have its own bathing facility.

### The Kiriyama Bath
The household bath at Kiri no Ue would be a private *ofuro* — a deep wooden tub, likely of fragrant **桧 *hinoki*** cypress, heated by a wood-burning stove beneath it. The most desirable ofuro are made from aromatic hinoki cypress wood. The smell of the wood and the steam together is one of the distinctive smells of a high-ranking household.

**The bathing sequence** — Japanese bathing is not what Western bathing implies. The tub is for soaking, not washing. The correct sequence:
1. Wash the body thoroughly outside the tub — pouring water over oneself, cleaning with care
2. Rinse completely
3. Then enter the tub to soak

The water in the tub is kept clean — it is shared by the household in order of rank, family first, then servants.

**Frequency:** Bathing became widely popular during the Edo period as a communal activity which was healthy, social, and relaxing. For a household of this rank, bathing would occur regularly — daily or near-daily, typically in the evening as the conclusion of the day's activities. Bathing at home in Japan is done in the evening, and is seen as an endcap to the day.

**The bathing room:** A small dedicated room adjacent to the main house, with the tub sunk into the floor or raised on a platform, the wood-burning stove accessible from below or beside. Steam. The smell of cypress and woodsmoke. In winter, the contrast between the cold of the corridor and the heat of the bathing room is extreme and welcome.

### For Shūmaru Specifically
Evening bathing is part of the household's settled rhythm — the day concluding in warmth and steam before the quiet of night. Shūmaru bathes in the household's order. He is not the first — Ume would manage the sequence, as she manages everything.

But there is another quality of water for him: the stream at the stone circle. Cold, clear mountain water over pale stone. He has reached his hand into it many times. He has drunk from it. This is not *misogi* in any formal sense — but it shares something with *misogi*'s impulse. The cold water of a sacred place, the body's contact with it, the particular clarity that follows.

## Part Two: 禊 Misogi — Ritual Purification

### What Misogi Is
Misogi (禊) is a Japanese Shinto practice of ritual purification by washing the entire body. It is related to *harae*, another purification ritual, and together they are called *misogiharae*.

Every year, many people take pilgrimages to sacred waterfalls, lakes and rivers, either alone or in small groups, to perform misogi. Mount Ontake, the Kii mountain range and Mount Yoshino are but a few examples of ancient and well known areas for misogi in Japan.

Shinto in particular uses a purification technique known as misogi which involves washing the entire body, often under ice-cold waterfalls in winter.

The principle: water — particularly moving water, cold water, natural water — cleanses *kegare* (spiritual pollution) and restores the person to a state of purity appropriate for contact with the sacred.

### Misogi and the Stone Circle
The stream at Shūmaru's clearing is mountain water — cold, clear, moving. Its source is above the stone circle. It flows past the *iwakura*, through the ancient enclosure, and continues down the slope past the shrine.

In Shinto terms, this water is already sacred — it has run through a site of *shinki*, has passed the *iwakura*, has been flowing through this ground since before the shrine existed. A boy who reaches his hand into it, who drinks from it, who sits beside it in the pre-dawn hours — he is in unconscious contact with something *misogi* was designed to formalize.

Gen'nosuke, when he understands the full nature of the site, will recognize this. Shūmaru's repeated visits to the stream — his instinct to touch the water, to be near it — are a natural *misogi* he has been performing without knowing it has a name.

### The Nagoshi no Harae and the Solstice
The ritual calendar document covers this in detail. The key point for the approach to the solstice:

The **Nagoshi no Harae** — the Great Summer Purification — is performed at the end of the sixth lunar month, very close to the summer solstice. It involves passing through the *chinowa*, the ring of woven grass, to cleanse the impurities of the year's first half.

Shūmaru passes through the *chinowa* at the estate shrine, with the household. One or two days later, in the earliest hours of the solstice morning, he goes to the stone circle and sees what he sees.

The purification ceremony and the vision are adjacent. This is not coincidence — it is the logic of sacred time. The purified person, moving through the right place at the right moment, encounters what is there.

## Part Three: The Approach to the Solstice — Daily Life

### What the Weeks Before the Solstice Look Like

The summer at Kiri no Ue is defined by:

**Heat and green.** The cryptomeria forest is at its densest. The canopy closes. The light that reaches the needle-fall is filtered, greenish. Outside the forest the estate is warm — the mountain location moderates the lowland summer heat, but the days are long and the afternoons heavy.

**Insects.** The sounds of the estate change in summer — insects in the long evenings, the particular sound of cicadas that begins and becomes simply the texture of the air. At night, frogs in the lower paddies, barely audible from the estate's height.

**The rice paddies below.** By midsummer the rice in the valley paddies is tall and green, the rows visible even from the estate's elevation when the mist has cleared. The farming families below are in their most intensive work period.

**The household's summer rhythm:** lessons continuing, martial training moving outdoors when weather permits — archery practice in the estate's open ground, riding in the early morning before the day's heat builds. The evening bath is particularly welcome in summer, the heat of the cypress tub meeting the body's own heat.

### Shūmaru's Days in This Period

**Morning:** He still wakes before the household. The summer dawns are early — the sky beginning to lighten well before the household stirs. He goes to the shrine most mornings, to the circle several times a week. The stream runs lower in summer — the spring snowmelt is long past — but clear and cold still, the mountain source unchanged.

**Lessons:** Continue as always, through the summer heat. The lesson room is on the north side of the house, which helps — the worst of the afternoon sun does not reach it. Gen'nosuke teaches with the same quiet attention in any season. After their visit to the circle together, something in the lessons has changed — not dramatically, not in any way the household would notice. Simply: the conversations go deeper. Questions arrive from different directions. Gen'nosuke occasionally pauses, as if reconsidering something, before answering.

**Riding:** Shūmaru's riding lessons continue, now including slightly longer routes — the head groom taking him along the track that runs along the estate's lower boundary, through the edge of the farming community's land. He sees the rice paddies from horseback. He sees the farmers at their work, bent over the green rows in the summer heat. He watches them the way he watches everything — without intruding, simply noting.

**The Nagoshi no Harae:** The household observes this together. The *kannushi* comes from the nearest castle town for this semi-annual ceremony. The *chinowa* ring is erected at the shrine's torii gate. The family passes through it — first the household head (in the father's absence, Ume assumes this function with her customary quiet efficiency), then Mitsumaru, then Shūmaru, then the senior servants.

Shūmaru passes through the grass ring. Left, right, left — the prescribed pattern, the ring high enough to require a slight bow of the head to pass beneath. The woven cogon grass smells of summer and something older. On the other side of the ring the shrine looks the same as always. But something in the air is different — or he is different, the passage through the ring having shifted something slightly.

He makes his offering. He stands for a longer moment than usual before the shrine.

That night he cannot sleep.

Not from anxiety — simply from a quality of wakefulness that is different from ordinary wakefulness. He lies beneath the yogi in the summer heat, the light thin cotton over him, the window open to the night sounds. The cicadas have gone quiet. The frog-sound from below is very faint. He lies awake and does not examine why.

He is asleep before the first gray of dawn.

He wakes before the first gray of dawn. 

He dresses in the dark. He goes out.
